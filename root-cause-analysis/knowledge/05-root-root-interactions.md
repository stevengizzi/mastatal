# 05 — Root-Root Interactions: Self, Non-Self, and Kin

Roots don't grow in isolation. They share soil with roots from the same plant, roots from related plants (kin), and roots from strangers. The interactions between roots add an entire social layer to the growth-direction problem.

---

## Identity Recognition

Roots possess "identity recognition" — they can distinguish between self and non-self, and between kin and strangers, and adjust their growth behavior accordingly [Depuydt2014].

Root systems develop differently when neighboring roots are present, and their responses vary based on:
- **Species** (same vs. different)
- **Genetic relatedness** (siblings vs. strangers)
- **Genotype** (even within the same species)
- **Self vs. non-self** (roots from the same individual plant vs. a different individual) [Depuydt2014]

**Key finding:** Roots can discriminate self from non-self even when contextual cues are controlled, indicating direct recognition via signals rather than indirect inference from resource competition [Biedrzycki2010].

---

## Range of Responses

When encountering another root system, documented responses include [Depuydt2014]:

1. **Overproliferation** — growing more aggressively (more lateral roots, longer main roots, more adventitious roots) when encountering a stranger
2. **Increased lateral rooting** — branching more in shared soil
3. **Accumulation in top soil** — shifting root mass upward
4. **Spatial segregation** — actively avoiding the other plant's root zone
5. **Root attraction** — growing toward related individuals

The specific response depends on the species, the identity of the neighbor, and environmental conditions.

**Kin behavior:** Some species constrain how far their roots spread when near kin, change flower production, or tilt leaves to minimize shading of relatives [Karban_UCDavis]. Sibling plants ("podmates") grown together tend to grow intertwined and stay similar in size; unrelated plants in the same pot compete until one dominates [HiddenValleyHibiscus].

## Proposed Recognition Mechanisms

Four mechanisms have been proposed for how roots identify each other [Depuydt2014]:

### 1. Root Exudates (chemical signals)
Roots actively secrete compounds into the rhizosphere — potentially over 100,000 different chemical signals [HiddenValleyHibiscus]. Root exudates are the primary candidate for identity recognition signals. In Arabidopsis, root exudates mediate kin recognition; seedlings respond differently to exudates from self, kin, and strangers [Biedrzycki2010].

In sterile conditions (eliminating microbial involvement), seedlings still showed recognition responses, strongly suggesting the signal comes from the roots themselves rather than associated microbes [Biedrzycki2010].

### 2. Electrical/Hormonal Signal Resonance
An "oscillation hypothesis" proposes that self-recognition occurs through frequency-matching of electrical or hormonal oscillations between connected roots. Self-roots achieve resonance amplification; disconnected roots show frequency mismatch and are identified as non-self [MDPI_RootExudates2025]. [confidence: speculative]

### 3. Root-Associated Microorganisms
Each plant's root microbiome is partially species- and individual-specific. Microbes and their secreted substances could serve as identity markers [Depuydt2014]. However, the sterile-culture experiments suggest this isn't the primary mechanism [Biedrzycki2010].

### 4. Physical Contact
In rice, some recognition responses require physical contact of the root tip with an obstacle or another root, not just chemical diffusion [Depuydt2014].

---

## Population Density Sensing

Beyond individual recognition, plants can sense population density. In Arabidopsis, single seedlings grown in the presence of many neighbors (via volatile compounds, without direct contact) developed shorter and less branched roots. This growth suppression was synchronized among individuals and transmitted to seedlings on the other side of a divided plate, suggesting long-distance signaling via volatile organic compounds [Crepy2015].

At high densities, volatile compound accumulation represses primary root growth and lateral root formation through antagonistic effects on auxin signaling [Crepy2015]. Plants are essentially doing crowd-sensing and reducing their root investment when competition is high.

## Implications for Root Growth Direction

All of this means that the tropism inputs from `01-tropisms.md` don't tell the whole story. A root tip is also receiving:
- Chemical signals from its own plant's other roots (self)
- Chemical signals from neighboring plants' roots (non-self)
- Volatile signals indicating population density
- Physical contact information from root-to-root encounters
- Signals mediated through shared mycorrhizal networks (see `06-mycorrhizal-networks.md`)

These social signals add additional "channels" to the mixing console metaphor from `04-decision-system.md`. And unlike the physical tropisms (gravity, light, water), the social signals are themselves responsive — the neighbor's roots are also adjusting their behavior in response to your roots, creating a dynamic game-theoretic interaction. ## Open Questions

- What is the chemical "vocabulary" of root exudates? We know roots produce 100,000+ compounds, but we've barely begun decoding which compounds signal what [Karban_UCDavis].
- How do recognition signals interact with tropism signals? Does the presence of a competitor change the weighting of tropisms? [confidence: speculative]
- How species-specific are these mechanisms? Most studies use a handful of model species; generalization is uncertain [Depuydt2014].
- Unraveling the exact nature of identity-triggering signals "would be a gigantic leap forward" — it hasn't happened yet [Depuydt2014].
