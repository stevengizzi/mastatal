# 06 — Mycorrhizal Networks: The Inter-Plant Communication Layer

If the root tip is a local agent making individual decisions, and root-root recognition is peer-to-peer communication, then mycorrhizal networks are the distributed infrastructure — the "internet" connecting individual plants into a community-level information system.

This is the layer that connects to Peter Wohlleben's *The Hidden Life of Trees* and the popular concept of the "Wood Wide Web."

---

## What Are Mycorrhizal Networks?

Mycorrhizal fungi form symbiotic associations with plant roots. The fungal hyphae (thread-like filaments) extend far beyond the root zone, dramatically increasing the plant's effective absorption surface. The basic exchange is: **the plant provides sugars (from photosynthesis) to the fungus; the fungus provides mineral nutrients (especially phosphorus) and water to the plant.**

When multiple plants are connected to the same fungal network, resources and signals can flow between them. A single fungal network can connect dozens of trees and understory plants.

**How the connection forms:** Plants release volatile compounds underground that attract fungi. The fungus wraps around the root, and the two organisms exchange small RNA molecules that alter gene expression in each other. If the fungus is beneficial, it signals "you can trust me" and facilitates the plant's growth [NatGeo_PlantsTalk].

---

## Resource Sharing

The most documented function of mycorrhizal networks is resource transfer between plants:

- **Carbon transfer:** Radioactively labeled carbon has been traced flowing from older "nurse" trees through fungal networks to younger trees that are too young or too shaded to photosynthesize efficiently [NatGeo_PlantsTalk]. This is the basis for Wohlleben's narrative about mother trees caring for their offspring.

- **Nutrient sharing:** Fungi are better at absorbing nutrients from soil due to their vast hyphal networks. They share these nutrients with connected plants in exchange for sugar [HiddenValleyHibiscus].

- **Water transfer:** Evidence suggests water can also move through mycorrhizal networks, though this is less well-documented than nutrient transfer. [confidence: inferred]

## Signal Transmission

Beyond resources, mycorrhizal networks appear to transmit information:

- **Defense signaling:** When one plant is attacked by herbivores or pathogens, connected plants have been observed to upregulate their own defenses before they themselves are attacked [NatGeo_PlantsTalk]. The signaling may involve small RNA molecules, volatile compounds, or electrical signals transmitted through the fungal network.

- **RNA exchange:** At the root-fungus interface, the two organisms swap small RNA molecules that alter gene expression in the other organism. This is a form of cross-species genetic communication [NatGeo_PlantsTalk].

## Implications for the Root Decision System

Mycorrhizal networks add a third scale to the root intelligence model:

1. **Scale 1 — The root tip:** Individual tropism sensing, hormone processing, growth direction (see `01-tropisms.md` through `04-decision-system.md`)

2. **Scale 2 — The root system:** Self/non-self recognition, root-root signaling, competitive/cooperative interactions with neighbors (see `05-root-root-interactions.md`)

3. **Scale 3 — The mycorrhizal network:** Community-level resource sharing, cross-plant defense signaling, nurse-tree dynamics

Each scale operates on different timescales and spatial scales:
- Root tip decisions: minutes to hours, millimeters
- Root system interactions: days to weeks, centimeters to meters
- Mycorrhizal network dynamics: weeks to years, meters to hundreds of meters

And each scale feeds back into the others. A nutrient pulse delivered via mycorrhizal network changes the chemotropic landscape experienced by individual root tips. A root tip's growth direction determines whether it encounters a fungal partner. [confidence: inferred]

## The Wohlleben Narrative vs. Scientific Consensus

Peter Wohlleben's *The Hidden Life of Trees* popularized the idea that trees are social organisms that communicate and care for each other through underground fungal networks. The core science is real — mycorrhizal networks exist, resource transfer is documented, and defense signaling through networks has been observed.

However, the field is more cautious than the popular narrative suggests:

- The extent and importance of inter-tree resource transfer is debated. Some researchers argue the quantities transferred are biologically insignificant in most cases. [confidence: inferred]
- The "altruism" framing is controversial. Whether trees are "helping" each other or whether the fungus is simply optimizing its own network is an open question. The fungus is not a passive pipe — it's an organism with its own interests. [confidence: inferred]
- Much of the foundational mycorrhizal network research comes from a relatively small number of studies, primarily by Suzanne Simard's lab. Replication and generalization are ongoing. [confidence: inferred]
- Some researchers (notably Justine Karst and colleagues) have published meta-analyses questioning the strength of evidence for inter-tree communication via mycorrhizal networks.

For this project, the important thing is the *architecture*: individual decision-making agents (root tips) connected by a distributed communication network (mycorrhizae) that enables community-level coordination. Whether the coordination is "intentional" or emergent from selfish optimization by each organism is a separate question — and an interesting one. [confidence: speculative]

## Relevance to Permaculture

Mycorrhizal networks have direct implications for permaculture design:

- **Preserving established networks:** Soil disturbance (tilling, excavation) destroys mycorrhizal networks. No-till approaches preserve them.
- **Nurse tree retention:** Keeping older trees in a system provides mycorrhizal infrastructure for younger plantings.
- **Guild design:** Understanding which plants share mycorrhizal partners can inform companion planting.
- **Inoculation:** Adding mycorrhizal fungi to plantings can accelerate network establishment.

This connects to the permaculture principle of "use and value diversity" — diversity in the mycorrhizal network increases the system's resilience and resource-sharing capacity.
