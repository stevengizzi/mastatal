## THE THESIS

**The garden and the village are the same design problem.**

Permaculture's three ethics — earth care, people care, fair share — are usually taught sequentially. But in urban rental life with young children, they collapse into a single design challenge: how do you build durable yields (food, community, resilience, skills) when your relationship to place is temporary?

The answer: **design for capacity, not infrastructure.** The most portable permaculture outputs aren't plants or earthworks — they're skills, relationships, preserved food, seed libraries, fermentation cultures, and the design methodology itself. The garden you leave behind is a gift. The village you build travels with you in the form of practice.

---

## CORE DESIGN TENSION: INVESTMENT vs. PORTABILITY

Every decision in this design maps onto a spectrum:

```
PLACE-BOUND ◄──────────────────────────► PORTABLE
(benefits the land/next tenant)          (travels with you)

Perennial food trees                     Seed saving skills
Sheet mulch / soil building              Fermentation cultures
Rain barrel installation                 Preserved food (canned, dried, fermented)
Worm bin (semi-portable)                 Plant propagation knowledge
Community garden plot relationships      NVC / conflict resolution skills
Neighborhood reputation                  Design methodology (the PDC itself)
Landlord relationship                    Cooperative organizing experience
```

**The sweet spot:** Systems that yield within 1-3 years AND build transferable capacity.

## RENTER'S ZONE MAP

Traditional permaculture zones assume ownership and permanence. Here's how they adapt:

### Zone 0 — The Rental Home
- **What you control:** Interior space, kitchen, countertops, windowsills
- **Yields:** Fermentation station (kombucha, kimchi, sauerkraut, sourdough), sprouting, indoor herb windowsill garden, medicine cabinet (tinctures, salves from KIS Botanicals training), food preservation pantry
- **Baby compatibility:** HIGH — all indoors, all safe, all portable
- **Portability:** 100% — everything moves with you

### Zone 1 — The Doorstep & Patio
- **What you control:** Container gardens, porch planters, vertical growing on temporary structures
- **Yields:** Culinary herbs (rosemary, basil, thyme, oregano, mint), salad greens, cherry tomatoes, peppers, strawberries in containers, worm bin
- **Baby compatibility:** HIGH — containers at toddler height become sensory gardens
- **Portability:** 90% — containers move; soil stays or composts into Zone 2

### Zone 2 — The Yard (Landlord Negotiation Zone)
- **What you control:** Depends entirely on landlord relationship
- **Conversation to have:** "I'd like to improve your yard. Here's what I'm proposing. It will increase your property value. When I leave, you'll have [X]."
- **Yields if permission granted:** Sheet mulch over lawn → annual beds. Fast perennials: figs (fruit in 1-2 years from nursery stock), blueberries (rabbiteye — fruit year 2-3, native to Georgia), muscadine grapes on fence/trellis (fruit year 2-3), elderberry (fruit year 2), blackberries (fruit year 1-2). Annual production: tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans, greens, sweet potatoes, okra
- **Baby compatibility:** MEDIUM — design paths, avoid thorny plants at child height, create "discovery zones"
- **Portability:** LOW for plants, HIGH for skills learned. Figs and berries are a gift to the next tenant.

### Zone 3 — The Community Garden Plot
- **What you control:** Your plot, your relationships with other gardeners
- **Key Atlanta resources:** 200+ community gardens via Food Well Alliance. Plots fill fast in early spring. Small seasonal fee. Community Foodscapes (CoFo) does professional edible landscaping in Atlanta.
- **Yields:** Food production + social capital. The garden is where you meet your village.
- **Baby compatibility:** HIGH — many gardens welcome families, education programs exist
- **Portability:** The plot stays. The relationships and skills travel.

### Zone 4 — The Neighborhood & Urban Commons
- **What you control:** Your participation, your reputation, your organizing energy
- **Atlanta-specific assets:**
  - **Browns Mill Urban Food Forest** — 7.1 acres, largest public food forest in the US. Free foraging. Volunteer opportunities. South of city center.
  - **Truly Living Well** — urban farm on Atlanta's Westside. CSA, tours, education, volunteering
  - **Atlanta ToolBank** — tool lending for nonprofits/community orgs (lending expanded to urban farmers and community gardeners)
  - **Metro Atlanta Mutual Aid Fund** — includes timebanking (exchange services using time, not money)
  - **Georgia Organics** — statewide network
  - **Global Growers Network** — connects diverse cultural communities through food growing (Decatur)
  - **Cooperative Atlanta** — hub for cooperative economics and alternative institutions
  - **Concrete Jungle** — harvests fruit from urban trees, distributes to food-insecure communities
  - **Wylde Center** — environmental education, community gardens (Decatur/East Atlanta)
- **Yields:** Access to land, tools, knowledge, food, and community without ownership
- **Baby compatibility:** Many of these orgs run family programming
- **Portability:** The network model travels. You can find/build equivalents anywhere.

### Zone 5 — The Bioregion (Wild/Public Land)
- **What you control:** Your foraging knowledge, your ecological literacy
- **Atlanta assets:** Atlanta is "the city in a forest" — massive mature tree canopy. Parks, greenways, the Beltline. Foraging opportunities (pawpaws, persimmons, muscadines, pecans in public spaces).
- **Yields:** Free food, ecological knowledge, nature connection for kids
- **Portability:** 100% — this is pure knowledge capital

## YIELD TIMELINE: What's Worth Planting for a 3-5 Year Tenancy?

### Immediate (Month 1-3) — Annual Production
| Crop | Notes for Atlanta (Zone 7b/8a) |
|------|-------------------------------|
| Leafy greens | Year-round with succession planting. Container-friendly. |
| Tomatoes | Long season (March-November). Cherry tomatoes in containers. |
| Peppers | Heat lovers. Excellent in Atlanta summers. |
| Herbs | Rosemary, basil, thyme, oregano, mint, lemongrass. Container stars. |
| Sweet potatoes | Easy, productive, tolerant of neglect. Slips from grocery store. |
| Okra | Southern classic. Heat-loving, productive, beautiful flowers. |
| Squash/zucchini | Prolific. Space-hungry but rewarding. |
| Beans | Pole beans on temporary trellis. Fix nitrogen. |

### Fast Perennials (Year 1-2 Yield)
| Plant | Time to First Yield | Portability | Notes |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|-------|
| **Fig** (Celeste, Brown Turkey, Chicago Hardy) | 1-2 years from nursery stock | Semi — can take cuttings | Southern tradition. Celeste = "Sugar Fig." Can grow in large containers. |
| **Blackberry** (thornless varieties) | Year 1-2 | Semi — root divisions | Primocane types fruit first year. |
| **Elderberry** | Year 2 | Cuttings propagate easily | Medicinal + culinary. Makes excellent syrup (medicine making skill from PDC). |
| **Blueberry** (rabbiteye) | Year 2-3 | Container-friendly | Native to Georgia. Needs acidic soil (pH 4.5-5.2). Pine needle mulch helps. |
| **Muscadine grape** | Year 2-3 | Leave on fence/trellis | Native. Self-fertile varieties (Tara, Carlos). Up to 8x more berries than regular grapes. |
| **Asparagus** | Year 2-3 (light harvest) | Leave in ground | 20+ year producer. True gift to next tenant. |
| **Strawberry** | Months | Very portable in containers | Day-neutral varieties for extended harvest. |

### Medium Perennials (Year 3-5 Yield)
| Plant | Time to First Yield | Worth It? |
|-------|---------------------|-----------|
| Pawpaw | 4-7 years | Only if you're staying 5+. But native to GA, tropical flavor. |
| Pomegranate | 3-5 years | Borderline for rental timeline. Beautiful ornamental meanwhile. |
| Persimmon | 3-5 years | American persimmon is native. Worth it as a gift to the land. |
| Pecan | 7+ years | No for a renter. But forage from public trees. |

## SOCIAL PERMACULTURE: BUILDING THE VILLAGE

### The Core Insight
The "village" doesn't form by accident. It requires the same design thinking as a food forest: you need to identify needs and yields, create beneficial connections, stack functions, and invest in the system over time. The difference is the "soil" is trust, and the "plants" are relationships.

### Atlanta's Existing Social Infrastructure for New Parents

**Cooperative Preschools (existing models):**
- **Inman Park Cooperative Preschool** — nonprofit co-op rooted in play, nature, and community (ITP)
- **Grant Park Cooperative Preschool (GPCP)** — Reggio Emilia-inspired, parent-led board, farm-to-school lunch, accepts from 3 months. ~108 families. Founded 2000.
- **Oakhurst Cooperative Preschool** — "seeks to fulfill the supporting roles once fulfilled by extended family." Outdoor-focused. Decatur.

These co-ops are proof of concept: parent-cooperative childcare already works in Atlanta. The question is how to build the informal version — the village layer that surrounds and extends beyond formal institutions.

**Innovative models:**
- **Heinsch House** — coworking + childcare space in Atlanta. "Work close to your baby." Intergenerational volunteer program with seniors. Community of working parents.

### The Village Blueprint: A 5-Layer Model

**Layer 1: The Kitchen Table (2-4 families)**
- Start with regular shared meals (weekly or biweekly)
- Rotate hosting. Simple food. The meal IS the infrastructure.
- This is Zone 0 of social permaculture — it happens in your home
- Stack function: shared meal = childcare cooperation + food sharing + relationship building
- Permaculture connection: this is where you share garden surplus, swap ferments, taste each other's preserves

**Layer 2: The Skill Share Circle (6-10 people)**
- Monthly skill exchanges: one person teaches, others learn
- Skills from PDC that are immediately shareable: composting, fermentation, seed saving, plant propagation, herbal medicine making, NVC
- Stack function: education + community bonding + capacity building
- Permaculture connection: propagation swaps, seed library, shared tool collection

**Layer 3: The Cooperative Ring (8-15 families)**
- Formal or semi-formal cooperative structures:
  - **Childcare cooperative:** Rotating care among trusted families. Each family contributes X hours/week.
  - **Buying club:** Bulk purchasing from farmers/wholesalers, split among members
  - **Tool/equipment share:** Lawn mower, pressure canner, dehydrator, power tools
- Governance: Use sociocratic/consent-based decision making (from PDC NVC training)
- Stack function: economic solidarity + childcare + food security + community resilience

**Layer 4: The Neighborhood Web (your block/street/area)**
- Beyond your intentional circle — the ambient community
- Actions: Know your neighbors' names. Share surplus food. Offer help. Show up when someone needs something.
- Atlanta's strong neighborhood identities (each ITP neighborhood has character) make this easier
- Permaculture connection: your front yard garden is a conversation starter and a demonstration site

**Layer 5: The Movement Ecosystem (city-wide)**
- Plug into existing organizations: Food Well Alliance, Georgia Organics, Cooperative Atlanta, local mutual aid networks, Atlanta timebank
- Contribute to the commons: volunteer at Browns Mill Food Forest, mentor at Truly Living Well, join AgLanta network
- Stack function: gives more than it takes from the local layers, while connecting you to resources and knowledge

### Governance: How the Village Makes Decisions

From the PDC's NVC training + sociocratic principles:

1. **Consent-based decisions** (not consensus): A proposal passes unless someone has a "paramount objection" — a reasoned objection that the proposal would harm the group's aim. This is faster than consensus and prevents the "tyranny of the minority."

2. **Rounds** for input: Everyone speaks in turn. No interrupting. Equalizes voice.

3. **Roles, not hierarchy:** Facilitator, note-taker, timekeeper rotate. No permanent leaders.

4. **Conflict protocol:** When tension arises, use NVC framework — observation, feeling, need, request. Address it early. The conflict IS the compost — it breaks down into richer soil if processed correctly.

5. **Regular retrospectives:** Monthly or quarterly "how are we doing?" conversations. Adjust the system, not the people.

### Failure Modes to Design Against

| Failure Mode | Prevention |
|-------------|-----------|
| Free-rider problem | Clear expectations. "From each according to ability" — but minimum contributions defined. |
| Burnout of organizers | Rotate roles. Rest is productive. Care for caregivers. |
| Gendered labor imbalance | Make it explicit. Track who does what. Men: do the dishes. |
| Ideological gatekeeping | Focus on shared practice, not shared ideology. You don't need to agree about capitalism to share a meal. |
| Social drama | NVC protocol. Address early. Accept that not every relationship will deepen — that's OK. |
| Over-commitment | Start small. One shared meal before a co-op. Let it grow organically. |

## PHASED IMPLEMENTATION: YEAR 1 THROUGH YEAR 5

### Pre-Arrival (Now — from Costa Rica)
- [ ] Research ITP neighborhoods (rental market, walkability, garden access, community character)
- [ ] Identify 3-5 community gardens near target neighborhoods
- [ ] Connect with Food Well Alliance, Cooperative Atlanta online
- [ ] Begin seed collection (start with what's available at Rancho Mastatal that's climate-compatible)

### Month 1: Arrive & Observe
- [ ] **Zone 0:** Set up fermentation station (sourdough, kombucha). Start windowsill herbs.
- [ ] **Zone 1:** Container garden on porch — herbs, greens, cherry tomatoes (if arriving spring/summer)
- [ ] **Zone 3:** Visit community gardens. Apply for a plot. (Plots fill early spring!)
- [ ] **Zone 4:** Attend one event each at: Browns Mill Food Forest, Truly Living Well, a farmer's market
- [ ] **Social Layer 1:** Invite one neighbor couple over for a meal. Just one.
- [ ] **Observe:** Map sun exposure in yard. Note soil conditions. Talk to landlord about garden potential.

### Month 3: Establish
- [ ] **Zone 2:** If landlord agrees: sheet mulch one bed. Plant first fast perennials (figs, blueberries, herbs in ground).
- [ ] **Zone 1:** Expand container garden. Start worm bin.
- [ ] **Zone 3:** Active in community garden. Growing food + growing relationships.
- [ ] **Social Layer 1-2:** Regular shared meals started (biweekly). First skill share hosted (teach fermentation or composting from PDC).
- [ ] **Baby prep:** Ensure all plantings are child-safe. No toxic ornamentals. Design paths for crawling/toddling.

### Month 6: Deepen
- [ ] **Zone 2:** Second round of planting. Muscadine on fence/trellis. Elderberry. Medicinal herbs for tinctures.
- [ ] **Zone 4:** Regular volunteer at one organization. Building reputation and relationships.
- [ ] **Social Layer 2-3:** Skill share circle formalized. Exploring childcare cooperation with 2-3 trusted families.
- [ ] **Preservation:** First canning/dehydrating/fermenting of garden surplus.

### Year 1: Harvest
- [ ] First fig harvest. Blueberry establishment. Annual garden in full production.
- [ ] Medicine cabinet stocked: elderberry syrup, fire cider, herbal tinctures, salves.
- [ ] Social infrastructure functional: shared meals, skill shares, beginning of childcare cooperation.
- [ ] **Decision point:** How's the neighborhood? The landlord? Recommit or start scouting.

### Year 2-3: Mature
- [ ] Perennials producing reliably. Muscadine on the fence. Blueberry bushes filling out.
- [ ] Seed saving from adapted varieties. Building a personal seed library.
- [ ] Cooperative structures solidified. Buying club or tool share operational.
- [ ] **Propagation:** Taking cuttings, starting new plants from your established ones — skill building for the next place.

### Year 3-5: Legacy & Transition
- [ ] The yard is a mini food forest. The social network is deep.
- [ ] **Decision point:** Stay? Buy? Move?
- [ ] **If moving:** Take cuttings, seeds, fermentation cultures, preserved food, the worm bin, all containers, all knowledge. Leave the perennials, the soil, the sheet mulch as gifts.
- [ ] **What travels:** The capacity to build the next one faster. The village-building methodology. The relationships that transcend place.
