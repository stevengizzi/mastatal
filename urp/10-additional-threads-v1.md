# Part I — The Ethos

## 1. The thinkers, and the ethos they point toward

You named six: Simon Sarris, Christopher Alexander, Henrik Karlsson, Nora Bateson, Sophie Strand, Maurice Merleau-Ponty. Different traditions, different registers — but the family resemblance is real, and it's worth naming what it is before the presentation.

**Simon Sarris** (*The Map Is Mostly Water*, Substack essays) writes from a New Hampshire homestead about making things by hand, restoring old buildings, raising children inside a rich visual-material world. His ethos: the modern screen-and-service life has atrophied the physical, aesthetic, and ancestral faculties of ordinary people. The antidote is *doing* — building, cooking, repairing, growing — not as productivity but as the basic nutrition of a human life. Sarris's patron saints are the craftsman and the classical humanist; his enemy is the abstraction that reduces a person to an economic input.

**Christopher Alexander** (*A Pattern Language*, *The Nature of Order*, *The Timeless Way of Building*) gives this instinct a rigorous theoretical spine. The "quality without a name" is his term for the felt aliveness of a well-designed place — a village square, a garden, a room that works. He argues that aliveness emerges from the relationships between parts: a window placed where light and sightline want it, not where the drafting grid demands. Pattern languages are generative grammars for aliveness: a catalog of repeatable-but-situated moves (like *north-facing outdoors*, *six-foot balcony*, *sleeping to the east*) that, combined in response to a specific place, produce living environments. His later *Nature of Order* extends this into a claim about the universe itself: "centers" that are more alive create other centers, recursively, fractally. A city done well is a fractal of living centers at every scale.

**Henrik Karlsson** (Escaping Flatland, Substack) writes essays on learning, attention, and the strange economics of a well-lived life. His operating move is to take ordinary questions ("how do you find the thing you should work on?", "how does a child become a good reader?") and treat them with the care usually reserved for research problems. The throughline: aliveness requires *attention*, and attention requires an environment that rewards it. He's a Christopher Alexander for the internal life.

**Nora Bateson** (daughter of Gregory) works with the concepts of *symmathesy* (mutual learning across living systems) and *warm data* (the relational information that's lost when you isolate variables for measurement). Her argument is that the modern sciences of management, education, and policy fail because they ignore the context in which any living process is embedded. You cannot understand a child by measuring them; you can only understand them by seeing them inside their family, their school, their neighborhood, their weather. Permaculture is already a warm-data practice; Nora Bateson gives it language for why the reductive measurement of conventional sustainability ("carbon footprint," "GDP per capita") is so shallow.

**Sophie Strand** (*The Flowering Wand*, *The Madonna Secret*) writes mytho-ecology. Her thesis: our ecological crisis is also a crisis of narrative. The stories we tell about humanity's relationship to the more-than-human world — mastery, extraction, linearity — produce the behaviors that destroy it. Her project is to find and retell older, mycorrhizal stories: hero-myths rewritten as mutualism, saints reimagined as composters, Christ as a figure of decay and soil. For the urban renter, Strand suggests: the move from industrial to permaculture life is not a lifestyle upgrade; it's a *re-enchantment*. You are joining a different story about what a human is for.

**Maurice Merleau-Ponty** (*Phenomenology of Perception*) is the philosophical bedrock beneath the rest. His argument: we do not perceive the world as disembodied minds receiving information. We perceive through our bodies, our skills, our habits of motion. A carpenter's hand *knows* the hammer. A gardener *sees* the soil moisture that an office worker misses. Skill is not layered on top of perception — skill *is* perception. This is why permaculture's insistence on doing rather than reading is not anti-intellectual; it's Merleau-Pontyan. The body-that-knows-the-garden perceives differently from the body-that-knows-only-the-keyboard.

### The synthesis

Laid side by side, these six converge on something coherent:

> **A human life is well-lived when the body, the mind, the home, the neighborhood, and the more-than-human world are in continuous, skillful, attentive, reciprocal relation. The project of modernity — specialization, abstraction, extraction, screens — has broken most of those relations. Permaculture is one practice, among several, for mending them.**

This is the ground you can stand on for the presentation. It is *not* "here is a design for an apartment garden." It is: *here is a recovery of a way of being that industrial modernity made difficult, and here is what that recovery looks like for a family renting a house in Atlanta with a baby on the way.*

The transition ethic in the URP is the Merleau-Pontyan move. You don't need to become perfect; you need to move your body back into skillful relation with the living world, starting with a sourdough starter and a rain barrel. The zone map is the Alexandrian move: the design is site-specific, fractal, alive. The cooperative ring is the Batesonian move: the household learns by being embedded in a web of other households. The knowledge-sharing layer is the Strandian move: you are not building a private fortress; you are participating in a story that others can carry forward.

### How to use this in the presentation

You do not need to name all six thinkers. Naming two or three is enough — probably Alexander (for the design logic), Merleau-Ponty or Sarris (for the body-in-the-world argument), and one more of your choosing. The synthesis above can be said in your own words in about ninety seconds, and it will land harder than any technical detail on the poster.

Consider an opening like: *"The question I want to answer today is not 'how do you permaculture a rental' but 'what kind of life does permaculture actually propose, and how far toward it can you move when you are renting, working in tech, and expecting a baby?'"*

## 2. "The future of work is terraforming"

The three tweets you saved converge on a single idea, and it's worth taking seriously because it may be the most important generational reframe in the room.

The argument, compressed: humans were not designed to sit behind screens all day. For roughly 99% of *Homo sapiens*' history, we spent our days outside, in motion, in skillful physical engagement with food, shelter, and other humans. The last seventy years of office life are a weird blip. The next seventy — if AI does what its proponents claim — could return the intellectual and routine work of office jobs to machines, and free humans to do the one thing machines cannot: *embodied, place-based, socially-embedded work on the living world*.

This is the same thesis Sarris and the permaculture tradition have been making from a different direction. What the AI boosters are beginning to realize is that the post-industrial utopia they've been promising — more leisure, more meaning — looks suspiciously like a permaculture homestead with better tools. Clean spring water. Food forests. Walkable cities. Regenerative farming. The difference is they're arriving at this destination from the direction of *more technology*, while permaculturists have been arriving at it from the direction of *less*.

The reconciliation, and the reframe that matters for the URP: **the "more technology" camp and the "less technology" camp want the same thing. Both are reacting against the same enemy — the 20th-century industrial economy's capture of the human body and attention. The terraforming thesis says: the payoff from automation is not more leisure to stream more content; it is the freedom to put our bodies back in contact with the world they evolved for.**

This gives the URP's use of AI (see philosophy.md §4) a much stronger footing. You are not using AI *despite* the permaculture ethos — you are using it *in service of* exactly the kind of life permaculture proposes. AI's job is to collapse the knowledge barrier that prevented most people from attempting self-provisioning. Your job is to use the freed time to build a life that your great-grandmother would recognize as a life.

> *Phrasing worth stealing for the presentation:* "We invented computers to do work. The first generation of computers let humans do more work, faster, from chairs. The next generation may finally be good enough to do the chair-work for us — which is the precondition for humans to do the work we were actually made for, outside, with our hands, together."

## 3. *The One-Straw Revolution* and appropriate technology

Both of these are already in the URP's DNA — worth stating explicitly for the presentation.

**Fukuoka's thesis** (*The One-Straw Revolution*, 1975): the proper response to agricultural complexity is not more inputs, more technology, more control — it is careful, disciplined *non-action*. Do-nothing farming means: no plowing, no fertilizer, no pesticides, no pruning. Observe what the land wants to do, remove the obstacles, and let it. Fukuoka's rice fields matched or exceeded industrial yields with a fraction of the labor and none of the soil degradation.

The underlying principle — and this is what connects it to Alexander and Merleau-Ponty — is that *living systems already know what they're doing*. The industrial impulse is to replace their knowledge with ours, which scales poorly and fails slowly. The permaculture impulse is to pay attention long enough to understand what the system wants, and then to *participate* rather than *impose*.

For an urban renter, Fukuoka translates as: **observation before intervention**. You do not arrive at the new rental and start building. You spend a month watching how the light moves, where the water pools, who your neighbors are, what the soil smells like, where the free fruit is falling. The temptation to immediately build raised beds, install a rain barrel, and sheet-mulch the lawn is the temptation to impose. The Fukuokan move is to observe until the design reveals itself.

**Appropriate technology** (the phrase traces to E. F. Schumacher, *Small Is Beautiful*, 1973): the right tool for a problem is the simplest tool that solves it, at a scale that the user can understand, repair, and replace. A rain barrel is appropriate technology. A municipal stormwater system with telemetry is not — not because it's bad, but because it is beyond any individual household's capacity to repair or replace, which makes its users dependent on a distant institution to live their daily lives.

The tight form of the appropriate-technology rule: **a tool is appropriate if the people who depend on it can, collectively, fix it.** A haybox cooker passes this test. A kotatsu passes this test. A sourdough starter passes this test. An iPhone does not. This is not an argument against iPhones; it is an argument for noticing which parts of your life depend on tools you cannot fix, and for making those parts smaller where you can.

These two traditions — Fukuoka's *non-action* and Schumacher's *appropriate scale* — are the technical-philosophical core of the URP. The haybox, the kotatsu, the bean teepee, the self-watering bucket are all Schumacher moves. The "observation before intervention" cadence of Phase 1 is the Fukuoka move.

## 5. The city reframe — urban density as permaculture advantage, not compromise

You asked: what unique advantages come from steering *into* urban life rather than retreating from it? This is one of the most important reframes the URP can offer, and the presentation should name it.

The standard permaculture narrative is a retreat narrative. The idealized outcome is a homesteader on owned acreage, growing food, cutting wood, raising animals, with neighbors who share the vision and a market day in town. This vision is coherent, beautiful, and has approximately zero purchase on the lives of the 85% of Americans who live in urban or suburban settings.

The URP's bet is that the urban setting is not a compromise position waiting to be escaped — it is, in several specific ways, a *better* site for permaculture practice than the dispersed rural homestead. Naming those specifics:

**1. Social permaculture yields compound faster in density.** A cooperative ring of 8–15 families requires that 8–15 families be within walking or short biking distance. In a rural setting, this means dozens of square miles and hours of driving per shared meal. In Atlanta ITP, it means a fifteen-minute walk. The same relational substrate that takes a decade to build in the country can be built in a year in the city. Shared tools, shared childcare, shared meals, shared CSA subscriptions — these all pencil out at roughly 5× higher velocity with density.

**2. Infrastructure that would bankrupt a household is pre-built.** Transit, libraries, schools, hospitals, water, sewer, electricity, internet. A rural homestead has to recreate all of this, poorly, at enormous cost in dollars and labor. An urban renter inherits it for the cost of a lease. This is a profound subsidy that homestead literature rarely names.

**3. The density of leftover resources is astonishing.** Dumpster-diving, salvage, Buy Nothing groups, Habitat ReStore, Craigslist free piles, bakery day-olds, coffee-shop grounds, arborist mulch, restaurant surplus. A dense city produces obscene quantities of high-quality material that middle-class households throw away. A rural homestead produces its own surplus but loses access to the surplus-of-strangers flow that cities specialize in. *The Urban Homestead* is effectively a manual for harvesting this flow; the URP's business/consumer loops in `05-consumption-loops.md` extend it.

**4. The diversity of specialists is irreplaceable.** In a cooperative ring of 15 urban families, you will have an electrician, a nurse, a cook, a teacher, a software engineer, a builder, a lawyer. These specialists can cover each other's weak points with an ease that no rural homestead community can match. Rural homesteaders tend to be competent generalists; urban cooperators can be a *coordinated specialty stack*.

**5. Public natural systems are free yields.** Atlanta's 48% tree canopy, the Chattahoochee watershed, the 4,200 mapped public fruit trees, the Beltline, the public parks — these are all ecological-commons yields that the urban dweller accesses without owning. A rural homestead owner owns their acres but not (usually) mature food forests, mapped fruit trees, or 22-mile trails.

**6. The transit of ideas is faster.** Cities are where discussion groups form, where alternative institutions emerge, where the Repair Café and the Tool Library and the Cooperative Preschool have the critical mass to function. A rural permaculturist reads about these; an urban one joins or starts them.

The reframe for the presentation: **the URP is not a second-best version of a rural homestead. It is a different and in some ways superior permaculture practice — one specifically adapted to the material, social, and ecological substrate of a dense modern city.** The Mastatal model of rural intentional community is beautiful and important; the URP is its urban cousin, and the cousin has advantages the rural tradition should learn from.

### A strong presentation line

> "The countryside is where permaculture was born. The city is where it has to grow up. Most human beings alive today live in cities, and the ecological crisis will be won or lost there, not on five-acre homesteads. Our design is a claim that urban rental life is not a waiting room before real permaculture begins — it *is* real permaculture, and it has advantages the homestead tradition hasn't fully recognized."

## 6. The traveling permaculturist — reconciling movement and embedding

The fear you're reframing is real: a person who loves travel, and a permaculture tradition that values deep-place commitment, seem to propose incompatible lives. Your reframe — *traveling permaculture nomads under a Manifesto for Responsible Global Citizenship* — is genuinely original and worth the URP developing.

A few observations that sharpen it:

**First, the URP already anticipates this.** The investment-vs.-portability spectrum in `01-philosophy.md` is exactly the conceptual tool needed: the sovereign outputs of permaculture practice are mostly portable. Seeds, cultures, skills, relationships, design methodology, NVC literacy, energy-audit literacy, foraging knowledge, medicine-making. The garden is a gift you leave behind each time; the gardener travels.

**Second, the indigenous precedent is real.** Most traditional human cultures were not sedentary. Seasonal migration, transhumance, multi-site inhabitation — these are the norm, not the exception. The permanent-homestead model is itself a modern invention, tied to enclosure, private property, and industrial agriculture. "Nomad permaculturist" is not a contradiction; it's a recovery.

**Third, what makes it *responsible* is the arrival protocol.** A tourist takes and leaves. A colonist imposes and stays. A responsible nomad does something distinct: she arrives with the intention to *learn the place* before acting, contributes according to local need and invitation, and leaves the place more resilient than she found it. This is a practice, and it has rules:

**A starter draft of the arrival protocol (refine in your Manifesto):**

1. **First month: observation only.** No garden. No infrastructure. Walk, ride, eat, listen. Find the paths, edges, nodes, landmarks, districts (Lynch framework — `02-systems-design.md`). Answer the bioregional quiz (nine questions from *Superbia!*). Learn the watershed, the dominant trees, the native foods, the weather pattern.
2. **Second month: build the kitchen table.** Invite neighbors. Host meals. Learn names. Ask what the neighborhood is already doing — what mutual aid exists, what gardens are underway, what losses the neighborhood is still grieving. Do not propose anything.
3. **Third month: ask permission.** Now you know enough to ask where help is wanted. Offer skills, not plans.
4. **Month four onward: contribute.** Build what the place asks for, in the form the place can maintain after you leave. Teach what people want to learn. Document publicly.
5. **Before leaving: transfer.** Run a handover. Document what works. Invite the next tenant. Name the cooperative ring members who will carry the practice. Leave seeds, fermentation cultures, and a written transition note.

**Fourth, this maps onto your specific situation exactly.** You and Jessie will be in Atlanta for 3–5 years. That's not a failure to commit; that's an opportunity to run the protocol well, generate a body of work and relationships, and carry the learnings and cultures to the next place. Document the Atlanta tenure publicly. Then do the same thing in Lisbon, or Mexico City, or Taipei — adapted each time. Over a lifetime, this produces not one deep homestead but a network of places where you have contributed, and a portable practice that deepens with each iteration.

**Fifth, the frame is scalable.** A generation of permaculturist-nomads practicing this protocol would distribute permaculture practice globally much faster than the stay-in-one-place model does. Every place gets the benefit of visitors who study it carefully, contribute carefully, and leave seeds behind. This is Solin's "knowledge is only real when shared" writ at the scale of a life.

### For the presentation

If you want to include this, the compressed line is:

> "The permaculture tradition has a sedentary bias that doesn't match most modern lives. Our design treats the rental as a station, not a failure — a place where we practice the design, contribute the practice, and then carry the cultivated skills and cultures to the next station. The garden stays. The gardener travels. The knowledge compounds."

The full Manifesto deserves its own document — a companion piece to the URP that universalizes the practice beyond Atlanta. Put it on your Year 1 publication list.

## 7. *Deep Nutrition* and *Salt Fat Acid Heat* — wisdom worth integrating

The two books you named point in different directions, and both are worth the URP absorbing.

### Cate Shanahan, *Deep Nutrition: Why Your Genes Need Traditional Food* (2009, rev. 2017)

Shanahan's argument, condensed: modern industrial food produces modern chronic disease, and the chain of causation runs through specific biochemical mechanisms — particularly the oxidation of industrial seed oils, the glycation of processed carbohydrates, and the depletion of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, K2) that traditional cultures got from organ meats, pastured animal fats, and fermented foods. Her "Four Pillars of Human Nutrition," distilled from traditional diets across cultures:

1. **Meat on the bone** (collagen, connective tissue, marrow)
2. **Organ meats** (dense fat-soluble vitamin content)
3. **Fermented and sprouted foods** (bioavailable micronutrients, probiotics)
4. **Fresh, raw plant and animal foods** (heat-sensitive enzymes, vitamins)

For the URP, Shanahan's most useful contribution is the specificity about what industrial food is missing — not just "eat organic" but *specific* deficits that traditional practices fix. This maps almost exactly onto the Ancestral Skills inventory in `04-inventories.md`:

- **Fermentation** (already in the URP) → restores probiotics and bioavailable micronutrients
- **Sourdough, not commercial bread** (already in the URP) → the long ferment reduces glycemic load and phytic acid
- **Bone broth and nose-to-tail cooking** → adds collagen and organ-meat nutrition that the URP currently under-weights
- **Pastured eggs, lard, tallow** → replaces seed oils (Shanahan is especially hard on canola, corn, soybean, sunflower oils)
- **Fresh garden salads and herbs** → vitamin-rich raw food, already in the URP
- **Raw dairy** (where legal/safe) → fat-soluble vitamins + probiotics

**Worth adding to the URP:** a short section on *cooking fats*. The URP has extensive coverage of food sources but doesn't name the kitchen-fat question. Pastured lard, tallow, butter, and olive oil; avoid industrial seed oils. This is a one-paragraph addition that substantially sharpens the nutritional argument.

### Samin Nosrat, *Salt, Fat, Acid, Heat* (2017)

Nosrat's book is the complementary half of the argument — not about biochemistry but about *sensory literacy*. The thesis: most home cooks don't cook badly because they lack recipes; they cook badly because they lack fluency in the four variables that determine whether any dish works. Salt controls flavor distribution and balance. Fat carries flavor and creates texture. Acid brightens and cuts. Heat is where transformation happens.

For the URP, Nosrat's contribution is a kitchen *pedagogy*: the argument that cooking is a skill that can be learned — not by memorizing recipes but by understanding four principles deeply enough to improvise fluently. This is the Merleau-Pontyan kitchen. You become a person who *perceives* the saltiness, fatness, acidity, and heat of a pan, and adjusts accordingly, without needing to read.

Two integrations:

**Skill transmission.** Nosrat's book is the text to recommend to new cooperative-ring members who want to raise their kitchen competence. It belongs on the URP's reading list — probably added to `09-sources.md` under a new category *Food and Kitchen Skill*.

**The fermentation connection.** Every major traditional culture solved the acid problem through fermentation: sauerkraut, kimchi, yogurt, kombucha, vinegar, sourdough. These are already in the URP. Nosrat's framework lets you see *why* they matter nutritionally and culinarily: they are the self-produced acid pillar that industrial food outsourced to bottled lemon juice and white vinegar. The URP fermentation practice is not an eccentric hobby; it's the family kitchen's acid supply.

### A combined principle

Drawing from both books: **A household kitchen that produces its own fats (rendered from pastured animals, cold-pressed from local olives or nuts), its own acids (lacto-fermented vegetables, vinegar mothers, sourdough), its own salt (purchased, but mindfully), and its own heat (a well-used stove, a haybox, a solar dehydrator, a kitchen-wise cook) is nutritionally and culinarily sovereign in a way that the average American kitchen is not.** This is a concrete, measurable target. Count the four production categories in your own kitchen annually.

## 8. Quantitative food comparison — supermarket vs farmers market vs garden

Your question: can we actually measure the nutritional difference between the same fruit (or vegetable) from different sources? The short answer is yes, partially, but with significant caveats that are themselves interesting.

### What *can* be measured at low cost

1. **Brix (sugar content).** A handheld refractometer costs $20–$40 and measures dissolved sugar content in juice or plant sap. Brix is not a perfect proxy for nutritional density, but in practice a higher-Brix crop correlates with deeper flavor, more minerals, more phytonutrients, and better storage life. A good garden tomato will read 8–12 Brix; a good farmers-market tomato 6–10; a supermarket tomato usually 4–6. This is a fast, cheap, kitchen-counter test anyone can run. The "Nutrient-Dense Crop" literature (Dan Kittredge, Bionutrient Food Association) uses Brix as its primary field metric.
2. **Mineral density via electrical conductivity.** EC meters (~$30) measure the dissolved ions in a pressed juice sample. Higher EC correlates with higher total mineral content. Combined with Brix, these two numbers give a rough index of crop nutritional density.
3. **Taste panels.** The most reliable at-home method: blind-taste the same crop from four sources with two or three other people. Score for sweetness, depth, complexity, texture, and aftertaste. This is not quantitative in a lab sense, but is more decision-relevant than most lab numbers.

### What requires a lab

4. **Specific vitamins and antioxidants** (vitamin C, polyphenols, carotenoids, flavonoids). HPLC or UV-Vis spectrophotometry required. The Bionutrient Food Association runs a crowdsourced lab program that tests samples for about $10–$25 per sample.
5. **Mineral profile** (calcium, magnesium, potassium, trace minerals). ICP-MS testing, ~$30–$100 per sample through agricultural extension labs or commercial labs.
6. **Pesticide residue panels.** Required if you want to compare contamination levels. ~$100–$300 per sample.

### The existing research

The Bionutrient Food Association's public database (bionutrient.net) has tested tens of thousands of samples and found what you'd expect: nutrient density varies wildly *within* categories. A supermarket organic tomato can be nutritionally equivalent to a garden tomato; a poorly grown garden tomato can be worse than a farmers-market one. The variable that predicts quality best is not "organic vs conventional" or "local vs far"; it's *soil health at the specific farm*.

The Rodale Institute's long-term Farming Systems Trial (40+ years) has consistently shown organic and regenerative systems produce higher soil carbon, better water retention, and (after transition) comparable or higher yields. Nutrient-density comparisons are variable.

### A practical URP measurement protocol

Quarterly, during growing season, compare four tomatoes from the same week:

1. Supermarket conventional
2. Supermarket organic
3. Farmers market (ask the vendor about practices)
4. Your garden

Record:
- Brix (refractometer)
- EC (conductivity meter)
- Taste panel scores (3+ tasters, blind)
- Cost per pound
- Observed freshness and storage life
- (Optional: one lab panel per year from Bionutrient)

Over a year, this gives you a quantitative basis for where to spend food dollars. Likely outcome: farmers-market and garden-grown will trounce supermarket on Brix and taste, with variable EC; supermarket organic and farmers-market will be closer to each other than people assume on nutrient density; the real quality axis is the grower's soil practice, which is not legible from a produce label and only emerges from a relationship with the farmer.

### The variable nobody talks about: time since harvest

The single largest quantitative variable in supermarket-vs-farm comparison isn't the farming system — it's *time since harvest*. Vitamin C declines 30–50% in leafy greens within a week of picking even under refrigeration. Tomato flavor compounds (volatile aromatics) degrade within 48 hours of chilling below 55°F, which is why a January supermarket tomato tastes like nothing. Most supermarket produce is 7–14 days out of the ground; farmers-market produce is usually 1–3 days; a garden tomato is zero. The nutrient-density literature consistently understates this because most lab studies are done on samples that have already lost much of what made them differ.

The practical consequence: the household that grows even *a small fraction* of what it eats captures a nutritional asymmetry that no purchasing strategy can replicate. A single garden cherry tomato picked warm has more volatile aromatics than a pound of January supermarket hothouse tomatoes. This is a measurable, not mystical, difference — and it's the strongest nutritional argument for Zone 0–2 production in the URP.

### Worth adding to the URP

A short section in `02-systems-design.md` under the Food System on *freshness as nutrition*, with the Brix / EC / taste-panel measurement protocol as an optional household practice. The Bionutrient Food Association crowdsourced program deserves a mention in `09-sources.md`. And the quarterly measurement ritual could slot into the metrics table in `07-implementation.md` — `Garden-vs-supermarket Brix differential` as a year-over-year tracked number.

## 9. Home mapping — tools for giving an AI "perfect knowledge" of a place

Your instinct is correct and the tooling is finally mature enough to do this well. The goal is not just a 3D model — it's a *semantically labeled* model the AI can reason over. A mesh without labels is just geometry; a labeled mesh is a queryable environment.

### Indoor capture

- **iPhone Pro / Pro Max (12 and later) with LiDAR.** The single best consumer tool for indoor capture. Pair with **Polycam**, **Scaniverse**, or **RoomPlan**-based apps (Apple's RoomPlan API produces architectural floorplans with doors, windows, and fixture placement automatically labeled).
- **CubiCasa / magicplan** — phone-based floorplan generation. Good for landlord documentation and insurance.
- **Matterport** — the professional-grade tool. Higher fidelity, cloud hosting, commercial licensing. Overkill for a rental unless you're running walkthroughs.
- **Valve Index / Meta Quest 3 with Scaniverse** — if you already own one, the higher field-of-view makes indoor capture faster.

Outputs: OBJ, glTF, USD, PLY meshes; IFC or SVG floorplans. USD and glTF are the AI-friendliest formats.

### Outdoor / yard capture

- **Drone photogrammetry** (DJI Mini 4 Pro, ~$750) with **Reality Capture** or **Meshroom** (free) for reconstruction. Overkill for a small rental yard; genuinely useful for an owned property.
- **Handheld photogrammetry** — same phone, walk the yard in a systematic grid, run through Polycam or Reality Capture. Good for <1/4 acre.
- **Solar Pathfinder** (~$300) or the free **Sun Surveyor** app — captures sun exposure patterns across the year from any point in the yard. More useful for permaculture design than any 3D model, because it directly answers *where will the sun be in August at 3pm?*

### The labeling layer (where most people stop, but shouldn't)

A 3D mesh alone is low-value to an AI. What makes it powerful is the *semantic overlay*:

- Room types (kitchen, master bedroom, south bedroom)
- Fixture inventory (each appliance with model number, age, kWh rating)
- Material composition (wall: drywall R-13; ceiling: R-30; single-pane windows)
- Sun exposure per room and per yard patch (morning/noon/afternoon/seasonal)
- Water flows (downspouts, slope, where water pools after rain)
- Electrical panel location, circuit map, amperage
- Plumbing access points, shutoff, hot water tank
- Soil test results tied to specific yard coordinates
- Microclimates (the patch behind the shed that stays warm; the frost pocket in the northwest corner)

Format: a JSON or YAML sidecar file tied to the mesh, where each labeled region has metadata. Some mapping tools support this natively (Matterport tags); most don't, and you end up writing a small custom schema. Notion or Obsidian can hold the labels separately and reference mesh coordinates.

### The pattern to build toward

> A single portable directory — `home-atlanta-2026/` — containing: floorplan PDF, 3D mesh (USD), yard photogrammetry (OBJ), sun-exposure dataset (CSV with month/hour/location grid), appliance inventory (YAML), soil tests (CSV), energy baseline (CSV), water baseline (CSV), and a README explaining how it fits together. This directory is your household's *digital twin*, and any AI you talk to about your household can load it and reason from it.

Do this in the first month at Atlanta. It makes every subsequent design conversation dramatically more specific and useful. It's also the thing you carry with you when you move — the digital-twin methodology generalizes even if the data does not.

## 10. The 100% self-production question — what would it actually take?

The honest answer for an Atlanta household without reducing consumption, using current (2026) technology:

### Baseline consumption (Atlanta averages)

- **Electricity:** ~12,400 kWh/year (1,035 kWh/month average Georgia residential — per recent EnergySage / Jackery reporting using EIA data).
- **Natural gas:** ~600–800 therms/year for a household that heats, cooks, and heats water with gas. (Atlanta piped-gas prices run 50–65% above national average, so the dollar impact is outsized even though therms are typical.)
- **Water:** ~72,000–100,000 gallons/year for a family of 3–4 (EPA estimate: 80–100 gal/person/day indoor + outdoor irrigation). Atlanta's own Department of Watershed Management pegs the average Atlanta family at about 6,000 gallons/month (8 CCF) — roughly 72,000/year.

### Electricity — 100% self-produced

Atlanta gets ~4.8 peak sun-hours/day averaged across the year. A 10 kW rooftop solar array in Atlanta produces roughly 14,000 kWh/year — comfortable 100% coverage with margin. Grid-tied with net metering is the cheapest path but not technically self-production (the grid is your battery). For true off-grid:

- **10 kW PV array** — ~600 sq ft of roof, ~$20–28K installed before the 30% federal credit ($14–20K after).
- **Battery bank:** 2–3 days of autonomy = 60–80 kWh of LiFePO4 storage. Current cost: $25–45K.
- **Inverter + balance of system:** $5–10K.
- **Total capital:** ~$55–85K before incentives, ~$40–60K after federal + Georgia tax credits. Lifespan: panels 25+ years, batteries 10–15 years.

Verdict: **technically and economically feasible for an owner-occupant today**. Renters hit this via Georgia Power's Simple Solar subscription (REC-matched, not truly self-produced but close) or portable solar for partial coverage.

### Gas — 100% self-produced (or eliminated)

True gas self-production means biogas digestion from food and animal waste, which requires meaningful biomass input and is practical only at homestead scale with livestock. For an urban household, the answer isn't "produce your own gas" — it's **electrify everything**:

- **Heat pump HVAC** (SEER 16–20, replaces both furnace and AC): ~$8–15K installed.
- **Heat pump water heater** (2–4× the efficiency of gas): ~$2–4K.
- **Induction range:** ~$1–3K.

Electrifying eliminates gas entirely and adds ~8,000–12,000 kWh/year to the electric load, meaning the full self-produced setup needs a **15–18 kW solar array + 80–100 kWh battery**. Total capital approaches $90–130K before incentives. This is genuinely a large investment but puts the household at effective net-zero operational emissions.

### Water — 100% self-produced (the hardest)

This is where the math gets uncomfortable. Rainwater harvest on a 1,000 sq ft roof at Atlanta's 50" annual rainfall yields about 31,000 gallons/year. On a 2,000 sq ft roof: ~62,000 gallons/year. Either falls short of typical consumption.

**Paths to closure:**

1. **Aggressive demand-side reduction.** Composting toilet (eliminates 24% of use), low-flow everything, no lawn, no car-washing. Can cut household water to ~40,000 gal/year. Then 2,000 sq ft of roof + storage covers it.
2. **Greywater reuse for irrigation.** With Georgia regulatory permission, greywater can supply 30–50% of outdoor irrigation, shrinking net demand.
3. **Well water.** Private wells in the Atlanta area run 150–400 ft deep, cost $6–15K to drill, yield 5–15 gpm. A well + rainwater + greywater is the standard off-grid water stack.
4. **Storage.** A 5,000–10,000 gallon cistern ($3–8K for poly, $15–30K for ferrocement) buffers multi-week dry spells.

Verdict: **100% water self-production requires either significant demand reduction + large roof + large cistern + greywater, or a well.** The well pathway is the most straightforward for a Georgia rural or semi-rural owner; within Atlanta ITP it's harder because many lots are too small, covenants restrict it, or municipal regs discourage it.

### The honest synthesis

- **Electricity 100% self-produced: solved technology, ~$50K investment, clearly feasible for owners.**
- **Gas eliminated via electrification: solved, adds ~$15–25K to the project.**
- **Water 100% self-produced: solvable with demand reduction + rainwater + greywater + possibly a well. Harder within city limits.**

Total capital to take a typical Atlanta household fully off-grid: **~$70–120K today, trending down ~5% per year.** This is within range of a home-equity line of credit or a green mortgage rider, and the operational savings (eliminating a $3,000/year utility bill) pay it back over 20–30 years even without incentives.

**The URP implication:** the rental phase builds the literacy, relationships, and measurement baselines that make this transition executable when you buy. You arrive at the owned-property conversation already knowing your kWh, gallons, and therms — which means the investment can be sized properly from day one instead of after years of expensive guessing.

| Community | Manual log or Airtable | $0 | Meals shared, shares hosted, hours contributed |

### The unified spreadsheet — structure

A single Google Sheets or Airtable workbook with these tabs:

1. **Dashboard.** Rolling 13-week and 12-month charts for every headline metric, compared against the Year 1 / Year 3 / Year 5 targets from `07-implementation.md`. One page, arm's-length readable. This is the tab you look at every Sunday.
2. **Baseline.** Month-0 snapshot of everything: kWh/mo, gal/mo, therms/mo, trash lb/wk, food $ total, food $ by circle, car miles/wk, shared meals/mo. Never overwritten — this is the reference you measure change against.
3. **Energy.** Daily kWh, by circuit if submetered. Monthly totals. Degree-day-normalized so seasonal effects don't fool you. Solar production logged separately. Net draw calculated.
4. **Water.** Daily gallons by source (municipal vs rainwater harvested). Indoor vs outdoor split where possible. Rainfall logged from the weather station. Rain-barrel state tracked.
5. **Gas.** Monthly therms. Degree-day-normalized.
6. **Food — dollars.** Each week's food spending tagged by source circle (1 = household-produced, 2 = cooperative ring, 3 = city local, 4 = Georgia, 5 = national/global). Formula rolls up to quarterly dependency-radius percentage.
7. **Food — yields.** Weekly harvest by crop. Annual totals. Calories / lbs / $ value at market-equivalent price (to quantify the yield from Zone 0–2).
8. **Waste.** Weekly lbs by stream. Monthly diversion rate (compost + recycling / total).
9. **Transport.** Weekly miles by mode (walk, bike, MARTA, car). Carbon equivalents.
10. **Garden log.** Planted, harvested, problems, weather, amendments. Photos. Becomes your place-specific agronomy notebook over years.
11. **Community.** Shared meals attended/hosted, skill shares hosted/attended, hours of mutual aid given/received, cooperative-ring members by date added, relationships by depth (Dunbar-style layers).
12. **Dependency radius.** Pulls from Food, Energy, Water, Waste tabs to compute what % of each category's spending/throughput sits in circles 1–5. The master chart.
13. **Projects.** Running list of engineering projects with costs, yield estimates, and actual results.
14. **Learnings.** Free-form monthly note tab. The qualitative warm-data half that the numbers alone miss.

### The meta-principle

A spreadsheet is not an end in itself. Its job is to make **invisible trends visible early enough to act on them**. If your kWh is creeping up, you want to know in June, not in January when the bill arrives. If your food dollars are quietly shifting back to the supermarket after a busy fall, you want the dashboard to show it.

Equally important: **the warm-data half matters as much as the numbers.** The Learnings tab is where you write down that the neighbor's dog died and the dinner rotation quietly stopped for three weeks. That's the information that explains why the "shared meals" number dropped, and without it the number is misleading. Nora Bateson's point again: the quantitative and the relational are not separable.

## 12. The unavoidable-waste loop — what to do with the stuff that can't be avoided

Every honest permaculture design admits this: even a disciplined household will accumulate plastics, foam, tires, batteries, electronics, and composites it did not choose and cannot fully recycle. The question is what to do with them. The answer is tiered, and it is genuinely more creative than "recycle harder."

### The tier stack (from the zero-waste tradition, sharpened)

**Refuse → Reduce → Reuse → Repair → Repurpose → Rot → Recycle → Landfill.**

Landfill is the last resort, not the default. The hard cases are what happens between "reuse" and "landfill" for materials you can't avoid.

### By material

**Plastic (especially single-use).** The best defense is a good offense — a cooperative buying club in reusable containers (`05-consumption-loops.md`) prevents most of this. For what accumulates:

- **Ecobricks** (ecobricks.org). Stuff cleaned, dry plastic into a PET bottle until it hits a density target (0.33 g/mL minimum). The filled bottle is structurally sound and has been used for bench seats, garden edging, and full wall builds in natural-building contexts. A family of four running ecobricks captures ~200–500 bottles of otherwise-landfill plastic per year. The filled bricks are also *portable capital* — they travel to the next home as material.
- **Precious Plastic** (preciousplastic.com, Dave Hakkens' open-source project). A cooperative-ring-scale investment: a shredder + injection molder + extruder costs $2–6K total and turns clean plastic into reusable products (stools, planters, tiles, rope). One setup per 10–15 families; shared like a tool library. The yield isn't just the products — it's *watching* plastic come out of the waste stream and into a planter on your patio. Visibility is pedagogy.
- **Terracycle Zero-Waste Boxes** (terracycle.com). Paid, mail-in recycling for categories nobody else takes (chip bags, toothpaste tubes, pens, plastic gloves). Expensive per box ($100–150) but cooperative-ring-splittable.

**Styrofoam (EPS).** Dart Container operates the largest EPS recycling program in the country, and their CARE recycling program has public dropoff sites in metro Atlanta (check caredfw.org / alliance of Foam Packaging Recyclers). For what doesn't go to Dart: reuse for insulation, seed starting (carve into trays), packing, shipping cushioning. Do not burn — the fumes are toxic.

**Tires.** Tires are reuse gold:

- **Tire gardens** — stack of three tires with soil inside is a classic urban potato bin. Lettuces, herbs, strawberries work in single-tire planters.
- **Retaining walls and earth-shelter foundations** — the Earthship tradition. Owner-occupant scale.
- **Playground and path surfaces** — shredded rubber mulch for a cushioning layer under a child's play area. Beware: tire rubber contains zinc and other trace metals; don't use for food-growing surfaces if possible.
- **Swings, tree guards, planters, shoe soles** (the SoleRebels model from Ethiopia). The cooperative ring could run an annual tire-redistribution event.

**Aluminum.** Easy to curbside-recycle; better to reuse first. Aluminum cans painted black are the absorber for the DIY solar air heater (`03-engineering-projects.md`) — a kitchen loop closed into a heating system. Aluminum foil folded into reflectors. Cans cut into tags, labels, garden markers.

**Glass.** Easier than plastic to close. Mason jars become the kitchen's universal container (fermentation, preservation, storage, drinking, lunch-carry). Broken glass tumbles smooth in a rock tumbler into "sea glass" for garden edges or craft. Bottles cut with a simple jig into drinking glasses or cloches (bell jars for seedlings). Cooperative-ring glass-bottle-exchange with a local brewery or kombucha maker closes the loop entirely.

**Batteries and electronics.** Best Buy and Home Depot take household batteries and small electronics free; Atlanta's Live Thrive CHaRM (Center for Hard to Recycle Materials, livethrive.org) takes everything else — paint, mattresses, styrofoam, scrap metal, fluorescent bulbs, textiles, large electronics. A single annual CHaRM run handles most household hard-to-recycle in one trip.

**Textiles.** CHaRM takes these too. Before dropping off: mend-first. Then cut to rags. Then compost if natural fiber. Then CHaRM.

### The audit-first principle

Before designing any waste strategy, **weigh and categorize your actual trash for two weeks.** Most households discover that 80% of their volume comes from fewer than 5 categories — and once named, the categories are usually tractable. You can't redesign what you haven't measured.

### The cooperative-ring angle

Most of the above scales nonlinearly with ring membership. One ecobrick practice across 10 families generates enough filled bottles for a garden-bench build. One Precious Plastic shredder serves 15 families. One CHaRM run per quarter, rotated among families, handles everyone's hard-to-recycle. The ring's waste stream, aggregated, funds and justifies infrastructure that no single household would build.

### Worth adding to the URP

A new subsection in `05-consumption-loops.md` titled **"The Unavoidable-Waste Loop"**, with the tier stack, the material-by-material table, and the audit-first and cooperative-ring principles. This is currently a gap in the URP — waste is covered for what's avoidable (compost, repair, reduce) but not for what slips through.

## 13. The Chattahoochee — how to inhabit a river

You're right to name this as a practice. Becoming a person of a place, rather than a person who lives in a place, happens through specific acts of attention paid to specific features of the more-than-human world. Trees, soils, birds, weather, and — especially — water. The river is where the bioregion narrates itself.

### Ecology — the shape of the living river

The Chattahoochee rises in the southern Blue Ridge Mountains of Georgia, at about 3,500 ft elevation near Unicoi Gap. It flows 434 miles south-southwest through the Piedmont (the geological step down from the mountains to the coastal plain), past Atlanta, forms most of the Georgia–Alabama border, and joins the Flint River at Lake Seminole to become the Apalachicola, which empties into the Gulf at Apalachicola Bay in the Florida panhandle. This is the ACF (Apalachicola-Chattahoochee-Flint) basin — one of the most contested river systems in the American Southeast.

In the Atlanta reach, the river is a Piedmont shoals river: bedrock exposures, rocky runs, pools. Key species:

- **Shoal bass** (*Micropterus cataractae*) — a black bass endemic to this basin. Fly-fishing Mecca.
- **Shoal spider lily** (*Hymenocallis coronaria*) — white-flowered aquatic perennial that grows only in rocky shoals of the Southeast. A federal species of concern.
- **Trout** — stocked rainbow and brown in the 48-mile stretch below Buford Dam, where cold tailwater out of Lake Lanier keeps summer temps in the low 50s°F (a rare cold-water fishery inside a warm metro area).
- **Atlantic sturgeon** historical presence; gulf sturgeon in the lower river.
- **Blue herons, kingfishers, barred owls, wood ducks** — the river corridor is a migratory flyway.

### History — layers under the river

- **Mississippian mound-builder cultures** (1000–1500 CE) along the river. The Etowah site, 60 miles northwest, is the surviving monument.
- **Muscogee (Creek) homeland.** The name *Chattahoochee* is Anglicization of a Muscogee word, most commonly glossed as "marked stones" or "painted rocks" — probably referring to pictographs on the shoals near Roswell/Sandy Springs. The Creek were forced west in the 1830s under the Indian Removal Act.
- **Antebellum cotton and slave labor.** The river powered mills at Columbus and Roswell. Civil War industry; Union General James H. Wilson's 1865 raid destroyed much of the Columbus industrial complex.
- **Post-war industrial corridor.** Textile mills, then paper mills, discharged directly into the river well into the 1970s. The river was effectively an industrial sewer in the mid-20th century.
- **Clean Water Act (1972) and recovery.** Water quality has improved dramatically since; still uneven. Atlanta's combined sewer overflows (CSOs) during heavy rain remain a chronic problem.
- **The water wars.** Georgia, Alabama, and Florida have been in court since the early 1990s over allocation of ACF flow — Atlanta's thirst vs downstream oyster fisheries in Apalachicola Bay vs Alabama industry. The Supreme Court ruled partly for Florida in 2018 and partly for Georgia in 2021; still unsettled.

### The stories we've told about it

- **Sidney Lanier, "The Song of the Chattahoochee"** (1877). The canonical poem. "Out of the hills of Habersham, / Down the valleys of Hall, / I hurry amain to reach the plain..." Worth memorizing the first stanza; it's the river's signature.
- **Stephen Foster** originally wrote "Way down upon the Chattahoochee" before changing it to "Suwannee" for scansion.
- **James Dickey, *Deliverance*** (1970). The river in the novel is unnamed but modeled on the Chattooga, a tributary — the mythos of "the river you go down and don't come back the same" became attached to the whole region.
- **Alan Jackson, "Chattahoochee"** (1993). The river as coming-of-age geography; the Atlanta suburban teenage summer.
- **The Muscogee cosmology** — the river as a living being, not a resource. Worth reading the writings of contemporary Muscogee Creek writers (Joy Harjo, among others) for this perspective.

### Current problems — where advocacy attaches

1. **CSOs and urban runoff.** Heavy rain causes Atlanta's combined sewers to overflow into the river. The city has spent ~$4B on the Clean Water Atlanta program and is still working. Individual action: rain barrels and sheet mulch reduce runoff volume; stormwater advocacy at NPU level matters.
2. **Development-driven sedimentation.** Every construction site that fails to control erosion dumps red clay into the tributaries. Georgia Soil and Water Conservation Commission is the enforcement body; reporting matters.
3. **PFAS / "forever chemicals"** from upstream industrial sources. Chattahoochee Riverkeeper's monitoring program is the current frontline.
4. **Flow allocation.** ACF compact negotiations ongoing. Political engagement through Riverkeeper and the Coosa River Basin Initiative.
5. **Climate-driven flow reduction.** Summer low-flows are getting lower. Adaptation on the demand side — which is what rainwater harvest and greywater reuse are — is the household-scale contribution.

### How to engage (ordered by ease of entry)

1. **Walk or paddle the river.** Don't research until you've seen it. Chattahoochee Nature Center (Roswell) runs interpretive paddles. The Chattahoochee River National Recreation Area has 15 land units with trailheads and free access. Start with Cochran Shoals or Sope Creek.
2. **Join Chattahoochee Riverkeeper** (chattahoochee.org). $35 membership. Monthly cleanups, Neighborhood Water Watch volunteer monitoring program (they'll train you to sample a tributary monthly). This is the *single highest-leverage* river-advocacy move you can make.
3. **Adopt a stream segment.** Georgia Adopt-A-Stream program — quarterly chemical and biological monitoring of a specific tributary near you. Concrete data that flows into state water management.
4. **Read Sidney Lanier, James Dickey, Joy Harjo, John Lane** (his *My Paddle to the Sea*). The literary and indigenous layer.
5. **Show up at NPU meetings** when stormwater or development is on the agenda. The link between land use and river health is direct; most residents don't testify about it.
6. **Use less water. Reduce fertilizer. Mulch.** The household's daily practice is also the river's daily practice.

### For the URP

This deserves its own short document — call it `10-bioregion.md` or expand `02-systems-design.md`'s Zone 5 section. The structure: one chapter per bioregional feature (the river, the canopy, the soils, the ACF climate, the Piedmont geology), each built on the ecology–history–stories–problems–engagement template above. Over 3–5 years this becomes your *place-knowledge book* — the written record of becoming of the place.

## 14. Neighborhood patronage as permaculture reconnaissance

This is one of the strongest ideas in your thoughts document. Let me sharpen it into a protocol.

### The premise

Permaculture design begins with observation. The traditional rural application is to walk the land for a year before building. The urban translation is to walk the *business ecology* for weeks before committing — because the businesses around your rental are the visible edge of flows you are about to join, and almost every one of them is running an open loop that a cooperative ring could close.

### The first-90-days patronage protocol

**Weeks 1–2: pure consumption.** Visit every business within a ten-minute walk. Coffee shop, bakery, corner market, restaurant, bar, hardware store, nursery, thrift store, salvage yard, dry cleaner, auto repair, barber, laundry, independent bookstore. Spend money. Say hello. Be seen repeatedly. Don't ask anything yet.

**Weeks 3–6: curiosity.** As the proprietor recognizes you, ask quiet questions:

- How long have you been here?
- What has changed in the neighborhood over that time?
- What do you make / sell / discard in volume?
- Who are your people — customers, neighbors, suppliers?
- What do you wish the neighborhood had that it doesn't?

You're listening for three specific signals:

1. **Material open loops.** Coffee grounds, burlap sacks, spent grain, cardboard, fryer oil, damaged product, end-of-day discards, broken fixtures, seasonal surplus, pallets, bags, boxes.
2. **Social open loops.** Quiet afternoons that could host a book club. A back-room unused on weekends. A notice board nobody curates. A bulletin of events the proprietor wishes she could publicize.
3. **Unserved demand.** "People keep asking for X and we don't sell it." These are the gaps a cooperative ring might fill.

**Weeks 7–12: proposals.** Only now do you start to offer things:

- "I compost heavily. Would you save your coffee grounds for me in a bucket? I'll empty it twice a week."
- "I know several other gardeners. Could we do a monthly grounds pickup so you don't have to hold them?"
- "Would you host a monthly repair café on a slow afternoon? I'll bring the organizing, you bring the space and the foot traffic."
- "I heard you mention you'd love a book club. Some neighbors and I have been reading *X*. Could we meet here Tuesdays at 6?"

### What this produces

Material yields (grounds, grain, bread, glass) and social yields (a neighborhood node, a regular rhythm, a relationship that converts in both directions). Every proposal you make is triple-win (you, the proprietor, the neighborhood) in the same way `06-community.md`'s landlord-negotiation protocol is.

### Specific Atlanta business types and their typical open loops

- **Coffee roaster** → burlap sacks (excellent garden mulch/weed barrier), coffee chaff (nitrogen-rich amendment), spent grounds (nitrogen-rich, slightly acidic — good for blueberries, bad for alkaline-lovers).
- **Brewery** → spent grain (chicken feed or compost activator), clean glass carboys, hops, pallets.
- **Restaurant** → fryer oil (soap making), produce scraps (compost), worn-out chef knives (sharpen and rehandle), linen.
- **Bakery** → day-old bread (Ch. 5 of *Urban Homestead* — croutons, panzanella, French toast, breadcrumbs), flour sacks (bulk food storage), large clean plastic tubs.
- **Nursery** → damaged bags of potting mix / compost / mulch (garden infrastructure), failed plants (propagation material), broken clay pots (drainage layer / shard mulch), seed library end-of-season.
- **Salvage and thrift stores** → tools, mason jars, fabric, books, frames, windows, doors, hardware. Habitat ReStore is the obvious one; independents are often more generous.
- **Construction sites** → scrap lumber, clean sheetrock (gypsum is a soil amendment), insulation scraps, pallets.
- **Arborists** → free wood chips by the truckload (ChipDrop.com is the aggregator). One call, one delivery, a garden's worth of mulch.

### The philosophical layer

What you're describing is the recovery of *patronage* as a relational act, not a transactional one. Industrial modernity abstracted the shopkeeper into "retail" and the customer into "consumer." The neighborhood economy that preceded it — and that still partly exists in European towns and the older American cities — understood that buying bread from the baker was a *vote* for the existence of bakers, and that the baker and the customer had mutual obligations beyond the transaction. This is Simon Sarris's territory; it's also the practical frame inside which the cooperative ring forms.

A new subsection in `06-community.md` titled **"Patronage as Permaculture Reconnaissance"**, with the 90-day protocol, the signal types, and the business-type table. This currently sits implicit in the "Errands Web" section but deserves explicit treatment — it's one of the most actionable moves in the first months at a new rental.

### Tool options

### The deeper purpose

A static map is a one-shot document. A good network visualization should be *live, forkable, contributable*. The version that matters is the one where fifty Atlantans can add nodes and edges over a year, because then the map captures knowledge no single household has. Kumu.io supports this natively (shared editable instances with role permissions). The Green Map tradition points the same direction.

Publishing this alongside the URP knowledge base — under CC-BY-SA licensing, with a "contribute your node" invitation — would be the URP's strongest single public artifact. It does at a city scale what Solin's "knowledge is only real when shared" principle does at a personal scale: it makes the implicit map of a place explicit and common.
