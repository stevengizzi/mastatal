The renter's zone map and the five integrated household systems: food, water, energy, waste, and transport.

## The Renter's Zone Map

Traditional permaculture zones (0 = home, 5 = wild) assume ownership and permanence. The adaptation for urban renters:

### Zone 0 — The Rental Home
**What you control:** interior space, kitchen, countertops, windowsills, energy consumption patterns.

**Yields:** fermentation station (kombucha, kimchi, sauerkraut, sourdough), sprouting, indoor herb windowsill garden, medicine cabinet (tinctures, salves from KIS Botanicals training), food preservation pantry, energy monitoring and efficiency baseline.

**Baby compatibility:** HIGH. All indoors, all safe, all portable.

**Portability:** 100%. Everything moves with you.

### Zone 1 — The Doorstep, Patio & Vertical Space
**What you control:** container gardens, porch planters, vertical growing on temporary structures.

**Yields:** culinary herbs (rosemary, basil, thyme, oregano, mint, lemongrass), salad greens, cherry tomatoes, peppers, strawberries, worm bin, vertical wall planters, herb spiral.

**Baby compatibility:** HIGH — containers at toddler height become sensory gardens.

**Portability:** 90%. Containers move; soil stays or composts into Zone 2.

### Zone 2 — The Yard (Landlord Negotiation Zone)
**What you control:** depends entirely on landlord relationship (see 06-community.md).

**Yields if permission granted:** sheet mulch over lawn converts to annual beds. Fast perennials: figs (fruit year 1-2 from nursery stock), blueberries (rabbiteye, fruit year 2-3, native to Georgia), muscadine grapes on fence/trellis (fruit year 2-3), elderberry (fruit year 2), blackberries (fruit year 1-2). Annual production: tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans, greens, sweet potatoes, okra. Rain barrel on existing downspout.

**Baby compatibility:** MEDIUM — design paths, avoid thorny plants at child height, create "discovery zones."

**Portability:** LOW for plants (gift to next tenant), HIGH for skills learned. Figs and berries are a gift to the next tenant.

### Zone 3 — The Community Garden
Atlanta has 200+ community gardens via Food Well Alliance (supports 300+ sites). Plots fill fast in early spring. Small seasonal fee. Community Foodscapes (CoFo) does professional edible landscaping.

**Yields:** food production plus social capital. The garden is where you meet your village.

**Baby compatibility:** HIGH — many gardens welcome families, education programs exist.

**Portability:** the plot stays. The relationships and skills travel.

### Zone 4 — Neighborhood & Urban Commons
Your participation, reputation, and organizing energy within the city. See 06-community.md for the full Atlanta organization ecosystem.

Key Atlanta assets: Urban Food Forest at Browns Mill (largest public food forest in US, 7.1 acres, free foraging), Truly Living Well (urban farm on Westside), Atlanta ToolBank, Metro Atlanta Mutual Aid Fund (includes timebanking), Cooperative Atlanta, Concrete Jungle (urban fruit foraging + Fruit Tree Map of 4,200+ trees), Wylde Center, Global Growers Network, Georgia Organics, MARTA, Atlanta Beltline (Southside Trail opening 2026).

### Zone 5 — The Bioregion
Atlanta is "the city in a forest" — massive mature tree canopy. Parks, greenways, the Beltline.

**Yields:** free food through foraging (pawpaw, persimmon, muscadine, pecan in public spaces), ecological knowledge, nature connection for kids. Concrete Jungle has mapped 4,200+ fruit trees across Atlanta and Athens.

**Portability:** 100% — pure knowledge capital.

## The Five Household Systems

Each system closes a loop. Each system intersects with the others. The integrated design is the point.

### Food System

#### Atlanta Climate Context
USDA Hardiness Zone 7b to 8a depending on specific neighborhood. Hot, humid summers. Mild winters with occasional freezes. Long growing season (typically last frost mid-March, first frost mid-November). Summer afternoon thunderstorms provide free irrigation. Red clay soil (acidic, iron-rich) — container and raised-bed approaches sidestep this. Abundant rainfall (~50 inches/year) but unevenly distributed.

#### Yield Timeline

**Immediate (Month 1-3) — Annual Production**
Leafy greens (year-round with succession planting, container-friendly). Tomatoes (long season March-November, cherry varieties for containers). Peppers (heat lovers, excellent in Atlanta summers). Herbs (rosemary, basil, thyme, oregano, mint, lemongrass). Sweet potatoes (easy, productive, tolerant of neglect, start from grocery store slips). Okra (Southern classic, heat-loving, productive, beautiful flowers). Squash and zucchini (prolific but space-hungry). Beans (pole beans on temporary trellis, fix nitrogen).

**Fast Perennials (Year 1-2 Yield)**
- **Fig** (Celeste, Brown Turkey, Chicago Hardy): 1-2 years from nursery stock. Celeste is the "Sugar Fig" of the South. Can grow in large containers. Semi-portable via cuttings.
- **Blackberry** (thornless varieties): year 1-2. Primocane types fruit first year. Semi-portable via root divisions.
- **Elderberry**: year 2. Cuttings propagate easily. Medicinal and culinary — excellent for syrup (connects to PDC medicine-making training).
- **Blueberry** (rabbiteye): year 2-3. Native to Georgia. Needs acidic soil (pH 4.5-5.2). Pine needle mulch helps. Container-friendly. UGA Extension notes rabbiteye types produce some fruit by year 2-3.
- **Muscadine grape**: year 2-3. Native to the Southeast. Self-fertile varieties Tara and Carlos produce up to 8x more berries than regular grapes. Train on a fence or trellis for vertical growing and privacy screen.
- **Strawberry**: months. Very portable in containers. Day-neutral varieties for extended harvest.

**Medium Perennials (Year 3-5 Yield — Borderline for Rental Timeline)**
Pawpaw (4-7 years), pomegranate (3-5 years), persimmon (3-5 years). Generally worth planting only if staying 5+ years, but all are native or well-adapted to Georgia and serve as gifts to the land and next tenant. Asparagus (year 2-3 for light harvest, then 20+ year producer — true gift to next tenant).

#### Vertical Gardening: The Renter's Secret Weapon

Vertical systems multiply production without modifying property. Ranked by renter-friendliness:

**Freestanding tower systems.** No mounting, no drilling. The Garden Tower 2 and similar rotational towers compost kitchen scraps internally while growing 50+ plants in ~4 sq ft. Fully portable.

**Trellising on temporary structures.** Pole beans, cucumbers, peas, muscadine grapes trained on freestanding or tension-mounted trellises. A-frame trellises fold flat for storage or moving.

**Railing and hanging systems.** Over-the-railing planters (no screws), magnetic planters on metal railings, hanging baskets from ceiling hooks. Best for herbs, strawberries, trailing cherry tomatoes.

**Pocket planters and felt wall systems.** Felt pocket planters attach to walls or fences and can hold dozens of herb and lettuce starts. Lightweight, removable, portable.

**Herb spiral.** A permaculture classic in miniature. Creates multiple microclimates in one container — Mediterranean herbs (rosemary, thyme) at the sunny top, moisture-lovers (mint, parsley) at the base. Can be built in a large container, fully portable.

**Hydroponic tower systems.** For maximizing indoor or balcony production. Countertop units for herbs, vertical NFT rail systems for leafy greens. Higher tech, higher yield per square foot, fully portable. Tech-compatible permaculture.

**Atlanta-specific.** The long growing season and abundant rainfall make outdoor vertical systems highly productive. Use shade cloth on south-facing walls July-August for heat management.

**Loofah sponges.** Growing your own dish sponge on a vertical trellis. Luffa aegyptiaca is a vigorous vine — perfect for vertical growing in Atlanta's long season.

#### Atlanta Food Sourcing Web

Build a web, not a single point of failure:

1. **Home production** (Zones 0-2): 10-20% of vegetable needs year 1, growing annually.
2. **Community garden** (Zone 3): significant supplement during growing season.
3. **Direct suppliers and CSAs:** Fresh Harvest (100+ GA vendors), Truly Living Well CSA, direct farmer relationships.
4. **Farmers markets:** 1-2 regular markets. Build vendor relationships. Ask about seconds and bulk pricing.
5. **Cooperative buying club:** pool purchasing power with your cooperative ring (see 06-community.md).
6. **Foraging:** Concrete Jungle fruit picks, wild edibles, the Fruit Tree Map.
7. **Grocery stores (for what remains):** prioritize cooperatives, minimize.

### Water System

#### Georgia's Regulatory Landscape

Rainwater harvesting is legal and supported in Georgia. Key facts:

- Property owners own all water falling on their property.
- Outdoor non-potable use (garden irrigation) is unrestricted — no permit required.
- Indoor non-potable use (toilet flushing, clothes washing) requires filtration and disinfection per state plumbing code (Chapter 15).
- Georgia income tax credit of 25% up to $2,500 for water efficiency equipment including rain tanks (HB 1069, 2010).
- Atlanta's stormwater ordinance (since 2013) requires all new developments and significant renovations to manage the first inch of rainfall on-site.
- The City of Atlanta has adopted standards allowing rainwater for potable use (with extensive treatment requirements).

**For renters specifically:** a simple rain barrel connected to a downspout requires no permit and no modification to the building's plumbing. This is the most renter-friendly entry point. Multiple barrels can be daisy-chained for greater capacity.

**Greywater:** legal but heavily regulated in Georgia. Not practical for most renters due to plumbing modification requirements. Manual greywater reuse (see below) is the workaround.

#### Renter-Practical Water Strategies

**Harvest:** rain barrel(s) on existing downspout. Atlanta gets ~50 inches/year rainfall — abundant supply. A single 55-gallon barrel per downspout is the minimal viable installation. Cost: ~$100-150 per barrel, plus diverter kit.

**Conserve:** mulch all container and garden soil to reduce evaporation 50-70%. Drip irrigation from rain barrel using gravity (simple timer and hose). Drought-tolerant native plantings.

**Reuse (manual greywater):** kitchen greywater reuse is zero-infrastructure. Save pasta water, rice water, vegetable washing water in a bucket, use directly on garden plants. Shower bucket catches warm-up water while waiting for hot water (2-5 gallons per shower). Laundry to landscape: if the washing machine drain is accessible and you use plant-safe detergent (no boron, no sodium), redirect the drain hose to an outdoor container during the wash cycle, then hand-carry to plants. None of this modifies plumbing. One important exclusion: blueberries. Greywater is alkaline from soap residues, and blueberries require acidic soil (pH 4.5–5.2) — greywater will kill them. Route blueberries to rainwater or tap water only. Greywater is fine for alkaline-tolerant plants: citrus, figs, blackberries, elderberries, and most other fruit and nut trees [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 6].

**Safety note for households with young children:** Standing water in buckets is a drowning hazard for toddlers — approximately 25 US children drown in buckets each year. Keep greywater buckets covered, elevated, or emptied immediately after use; never leave a water-filled bucket unattended on the floor [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 6].

**Cycle:** rain → barrel → garden → food → kitchen scraps → compost → soil → garden. The loop closes.

### Energy System

#### The Reality Check

HVAC is 40-50% of a Southern home's energy bill. This is the biggest lever. A typical US household uses about 10,500 kWh/year.

The degrowth framing isn't about eliminating technology — it's about eliminating waste. A well-insulated rental with efficient appliances, strategic passive cooling, and renewable offsets can maintain a modern lifestyle while dramatically reducing its footprint.

#### Reduce (Renter-Compatible)

Thermal curtains on all windows (especially south and west-facing) — heavy, insulated curtains reduce heat gain 33% in summer and heat loss 25% in winter. Removable window insulation film (shrink-fit kits, ~$5-10/window). Draft stoppers on doors. Strategic fan use over AC (ceiling fans reduce perceived temperature 4-6°F). LED bulbs throughout. Smart power strips to eliminate phantom loads. Retained-heat cooking (bring to boil, insulate, finish with no energy input — see 03-engineering-projects.md for the haybox). Batch cooking to reduce oven use. Line-drying clothes. Heated table (kotatsu) heats the person, not the room — see 03-engineering-projects.md.

#### Monitor

A simple energy monitor (Kill-A-Watt meter or whole-house monitor) makes invisible consumption visible. Track kWh monthly. Set targets. Monitoring alone typically reduces consumption 5-15%.

#### Upgrade (Georgia Power Programs for Renters)

**EASE Program (Energy Assistance for Savings and Efficiency):** free energy efficiency improvements for income-qualifying customers. Renters can participate with landlord's written consent. Approved measures: attic insulation, air sealing, duct sealing, LED lighting, HVAC servicing.

**Home Energy Improvement Program (HEIP):** rebates up to 50% for efficiency upgrades. Renter participation with landlord consent. Biggest ROI in Southern homes: attic insulation plus air sealing.

**Home Energy Assessment:** free. Provides specific recommendations and identifies rebate-eligible improvements.

#### Generate (Renter-Compatible)

**Simple Solar:** Georgia Power subscription program available to all customers including renters. Match 50% or 100% of monthly usage via Renewable Energy Credits (RECs) for 1.25¢/kWh additional. A customer using 1,000 kWh/month pays ~$12.50/month for 100% matching. Not true net-zero, but a step toward renewable sourcing.

**Portable solar:** 200-400W portable solar panels on balcony/patio + 1-2 kWh LiFePO4 battery + small inverter. Powers all devices, LED lighting, small fans. Fully portable, no building modification. Not enough for HVAC, but meaningful for reducing grid dependence.

The real value of portable solar isn't energy independence — it's energy literacy. Running a portion of your household on visible, tangible solar power changes your relationship to energy consumption. See the sun-to-use pipeline directly.

### Waste System

#### Composting Options for Renters

**Indoor worm bin (vermicomposting).** Most renter-friendly. Stackable worm bin under the kitchen sink or on a balcony processes kitchen scraps into worm castings — premium soil amendment. Odorless when maintained correctly. Fully portable. Low maintenance. Baby-safe with secure lid.

**Bokashi fermentation.** Anaerobic composting in a sealed bucket. Processes meat, dairy, and cooked food that traditional composting can't handle. The fermented material can be buried in a container garden or added to a compost pile. Compact, odorless, indoor-compatible.

**CompostNow (formerly Compostwheels).** Atlanta-based residential composting pickup service. Weekly or biweekly collection of kitchen scraps in a provided pail. After four months, members can receive up to 50 lbs of finished compost annually. Serves ITP Atlanta. $20-35/month. Partners with Food Well Alliance and the City of East Point on USDA-funded community composting programs.

**Food scrap drop-off sites.** Urban Food Forest at Browns Mill and Wylde Center accept food scraps.

**Backyard composting regulations.** In Georgia, backyard composting is generally exempt from state regulations. Turner Environmental Law Clinic has a fact sheet on small-scale composting. UGA Extension provides detailed guides. Food Well Alliance curates Atlanta-specific composting resources.

#### Broader Waste Reduction

**Zero-waste kitchen design.** Bulk buying through a cooperative buying club, reusable containers, food preservation (canning, dehydrating, fermenting), nose-to-tail cooking. Near-zero food waste household.

**Goodr.** Atlanta-based edible-food recovery service for businesses, plus commercial composting pickups.

**Repair culture.** Atlanta lacks a regular repair café — a gap to fill within the skill share circle (see 06-community.md).

### Transport System

#### MARTA
Heavy rail system with four lines serving key ITP neighborhoods. Buses serve a wider network. MARTA is bike-friendly: bikes welcome on all trains at all times. Every bus has a front-mounted bike rack holding two bikes.

**New MARTA Rapid A-Line BRT** targeting spring/summer 2026 debut. Links downtown Atlanta to Summerhill, Peoplestown, and the Beltline's Southside Trail via a 14-stop, five-mile loop with dedicated bus lanes, electric 60-foot buses, and off-board payment.

#### Atlanta Beltline
22-mile loop of multi-use trails, parks, and transit connecting 45 neighborhoods. Southside Trail sections opening first half of 2026. Transforming car-free viability ITP.

#### Cycling Infrastructure (Improving Rapidly)
Protected bike lanes debuting 2026: Midtown (Juniper Complete Street Project), Avondale Estates (North Avondale/East College), Decatur (West Howard Avenue Cycle Track), Boulevard from Grant Park south. Propel ATL (formerly Atlanta Bicycle Coalition) advocates for continued expansion.

#### E-cargo Bikes
Potentially transformative for a family with young children — replacing a second car for grocery runs, community garden trips, library visits, neighborhood errands. Atlanta's relatively flat ITP terrain makes e-biking practical. Combine with MARTA for longer trips. Ideal candidate for cooperative ring sharing (see 06-community.md).

#### The Permaculture Transportation Design Principle
Choose housing location first based on proximity to: MARTA station, community garden, grocery access, and key community nodes. **The most sustainable transportation decision is a short trip or no trip at all.** When evaluating ITP neighborhoods, map walking and biking distances to the resources that matter most.

| Medicine making workshop | Herbal pharmacy | — | — | — | Healing community | — |

The goal of systems design is that **no activity serves only one purpose.** Every intervention should yield across multiple systems simultaneously.
