# 07 — Implementation

The phased rollout from pre-arrival through year 5, with specific actions across all systems.

---

## Phase 0: Pre-Arrival (Now, from Costa Rica)

**Research**
- [ ] ITP Atlanta neighborhoods, prioritizing: MARTA proximity, community garden access, walkability/bikeability, neighborhood character
- [ ] Identify 3-5 community gardens near target neighborhoods via Food Well Alliance
- [ ] Review Georgia Power programs (EASE, HEIP, Simple Solar)
- [ ] Check CompostNow service area for target neighborhoods
- [ ] Review Atlanta's cooperative preschools (GPCP, Inman Park, Oakhurst) for future childcare planning

**Connect**
- [ ] Online: Food Well Alliance, Cooperative Atlanta, Propel ATL, local mutual aid
- [ ] Subscribe to TheLastFarm, Low-tech Magazine, Drawdown Georgia

**Prepare**
- [ ] Begin seed collection (start with what's climate-compatible for zone 7b/8a)
- [ ] Purchase core PDC knowledge books for reference (The Urban Homestead, Superbia, Ben Falk's Resilient Farm and Homestead)

## Phase 1: Month 1 (Arrive & Observe)

### Zone 0 (Home)
- [ ] Fermentation station: start sourdough starter, kombucha SCOBY, first batch of sauerkraut
- [ ] Windowsill herbs: basil, parsley, chives, mint
- [ ] Energy audit: inventory all appliances. Acquire Kill-A-Watt meter. Set baseline for monthly kWh.
- [ ] Start the compost-bin-from-garbage-can (*Urban Homestead* Essential Project 1) and the two-tote worm bin (Essential Project 2) on arrival week — both are same-day projects. [Ch. 2]

### Zone 1 (Doorstep/Patio)
- [ ] Container garden: herbs, salad greens, cherry tomatoes (if arriving spring/summer)
- [ ] Build 2–3 DIY self-watering containers from salvaged 5-gallon buckets as the first outdoor-growing infrastructure. One hour each; $0 if buckets are scavenged from restaurants, ~$15 if purchased. Initial plantings: basil, patio cherry tomato, Italian parsley. [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 2, Essential Project 5]
- [ ] Start worm bin
- [ ] Research vertical systems — pick one to install in month 2

### Zone 3 (Community Garden)
- [ ] Visit 3 community gardens. Talk to existing gardeners.
- [ ] Apply for a plot (plots fill in early spring — urgent if arriving then)

### Zone 4 (Neighborhood/Commons)
- [ ] Attend one event at: Browns Mill Food Forest, Truly Living Well, farmers market, Concrete Jungle fruit pick
- [ ] Map walking/biking routes to key destinations

### Social (Layer 1)
- [ ] Invite one neighbor couple over for a meal. Just one.
- [ ] Say hi to immediate neighbors, learn names

### Waste
- [ ] Sign up for CompostNow pickup
- [ ] Establish zero-waste kitchen practices

### Transport
- [ ] Map primary routes by bike/MARTA/walk
- [ ] First bike ride on the Beltline

### Observe
- [ ] Map sun exposure in yard (take photos at 9am, noon, 3pm, 6pm)
- [ ] Note soil conditions, drainage patterns
- [ ] **Soil lead test at move-in** if growing anything in ground (Zone 2) at an older rental. Lead is the main Atlanta-renter concern given housing stock that predates the 1978 lead-paint ban. ~$30 through county health department. High-lead results aren't game-over — fruiting crops (tomatoes, peppers, squash, fig) stay safe, root veg is scrubbed and peeled, leafy greens move to raised beds or containers with imported soil. Heavy organic matter (compost, mulch) *reduces* lead plant uptake. Wash all produce in 1% vinegar water (University of Minnesota Extension) to remove surface lead. [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 2, "Why You Might Test Your Soil"]
- [ ] Initial conversation with landlord about garden potential (just planting the idea)
- [ ] Local sourcing: identify the first 5 purchases to redirect to local sources (coffee, bread, eggs, produce, soap)

## Phase 2: Month 3-6 (Establish)

### Zone 2 (Yard — if landlord agrees)
- [ ] **Sheet-mulch a section of lawn as the first intervention.** No dig, no till. Wet cardboard or 4–6 sheets of newspaper as weed barrier, plus 5 inches of organic matter on top. Plant directly through by cutting X-slits through the barrier and tucking plants into the soil below. Lower-labor than digging and *improves* the ground rather than stripping it — strong for landlord triple-win framing. [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 2, "Sheet Mulching"]
- [ ] Plant first fast perennials (fig, blueberry, elderberry)
- [ ] Install rain barrel on existing downspout
- [ ] Muscadine on fence/trellis for vertical food production

### Zone 1
- [ ] Expand container garden
- [ ] First vertical system operational (tower or trellis)
- [ ] Herb spiral established

### Zone 3
- [ ] Active in community garden. Build relationships.
- [ ] Contribute surplus from home garden to community gardeners

### Social (Layers 1-2)
- [ ] Biweekly shared meals established
- [ ] First skill share hosted — teach fermentation or composting from PDC
- [ ] Identify 2-3 families who might form the cooperative ring

### Energy
- [ ] Apply for Georgia Power energy assessment (with landlord consent)
- [ ] Install thermal curtains, LED bulbs, smart power strips
- [ ] Weatherstripping on doors and windows
- [ ] Consider Simple Solar subscription
- [ ] Build first engineering project (haybox cooker is easiest starting point)

### Water
- [ ] Rain barrel installed and functional
- [ ] Mulch all garden soil
- [ ] Begin manual greywater practice: bucket in the shower catches warm-up water; kitchen vegetable-wash water goes to house plants; pasta/rice water to outdoor plants. **Critical safety note for households with young children: approximately 25 US kids drown in buckets each year. Don't leave any water-filled bucket unattended. Dump immediately after each use or store inverted when not actively filling.** [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 6, "Non-Invasive Greywater Methodologies"]

### Waste
- [ ] Worm bin mature and productive
- [ ] Bokashi bucket for meat/dairy scraps
- [ ] Zero-waste shopping patterns established

### Baby/family prep
- [ ] Ensure all plantings are child-safe
- [ ] No toxic ornamentals
- [ ] Design paths for crawling/toddling

### Local sourcing
- [ ] Fresh Harvest subscription active
- [ ] Regular farmers market attendance
- [ ] First direct farmer relationships
- [ ] Local credit union opened

## Phase 5: Year 3-5 (Legacy & Transition)

### The mature system
- [ ] The yard is a mini food forest
- [ ] The social network is deep
- [ ] Household operates as an integrated system across all five areas (food, water, energy, waste, transport)
- [ ] Dependency radius: 70%+ in circles 1-4

### Decision point
- [ ] Stay? Buy? Move?
- [ ] If the rental has become a home, explore purchase
- [ ] If moving to a different city: extract everything portable, leave a documented gift

### If moving
**Take:**
- [ ] Seeds (adapted varieties)
- [ ] Fermentation cultures (sourdough, kombucha, kefir, etc.)
- [ ] Preserved food (canned, dried, fermented)
- [ ] Worm bin
- [ ] All containers and vertical systems
- [ ] Portable solar system
- [ ] Energy monitoring tools
- [ ] All knowledge and design methodology

**Leave (as gifts):**
- [ ] Perennial plants (fig, blueberry, muscadine, elderberry)
- [ ] Built soil and sheet mulch
- [ ] Rain barrel
- [ ] Documentation for next tenant — how to maintain what you built
- [ ] Connections to the cooperative ring, Food Well Alliance, CompostNow

### What travels
- [ ] The capacity to build the next one faster
- [ ] The village-building methodology
- [ ] The relationships that transcend place
- [ ] Published design (shared openly for others to use)

## Metrics to Track

Quantitative metrics keep the transition honest. Track quarterly:

| Metric | Year 1 Target | Year 3 Target | Year 5 Target |
|--------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Monthly kWh | -15-25% vs baseline | -30-40% | -40-50% |
| Food dollars in circles 1-3 | 30%+ | 50%+ | 70%+ |
| Food dollars in Georgia (circles 1-4) | 50%+ | 65%+ | 80%+ |
| Trash volume | -40% vs typical | -60% | -70% |
| Car trips per week | -20% vs baseline | -50% | -70% |
| Water harvested from rain | 50+ gal/yr | 500+ gal/yr | 1000+ gal/yr |
| Skill shares hosted/attended | 12 | 24+ | ongoing rhythm |
| Cooperative ring size | 2-4 families | 8-15 families | stable + expanding network |
| Documented knowledge shared | 1 public resource | 3+ public resources | active teaching |
| Sourdough starter age | Baking-ready (~2 weeks of daily feedings) | Established culture | Local-terroir character; portable multi-year asset |

**Sourdough starter age.** A new starter can be baking-ready in two weeks of daily feedings. By Month 3, a healthy culture is established; by Year 1, the starter has local-terroir character. A starter is a portable multi-year asset: bring it to Atlanta, maintain through the rental period, carry it to the eventual owned property.

The numbers are approximate. The direction is the point.

## The Living Document Principle

This timeline is a scaffold, not a prescription. Real implementation will rearrange, adapt, and innovate. The principles that hold:

- Start with what requires no permission
- Add one system at a time — don't try to do everything in month 1
- Measure what matters; adjust based on data
- Document as you go (for yourselves, your landlord, the next tenant, the wider community)
- Every year, review and redesign. The original plan is not sacred.
- The transition ethic governs: every move toward more integrated, more local, more sovereign living is a success — even if the pace is slower than the ideal.
