# 03 — Engineering Projects

Creative engineering solutions that combine ancestral wisdom with modern knowledge, sourced locally wherever possible.

## The Low-Tech Magazine Philosophy

Low-tech Magazine (solar.lowtechmagazine.com) — a solar-powered website that sometimes goes offline — articulates the principle: *"interesting possibilities arise when you combine old technology with new knowledge and new materials, or when you apply old concepts and traditional knowledge to modern technology."*

The site itself is proof of concept: it runs on a mini-computer with 1-2.5 watts of power, supplied by a small off-grid solar PV system on the author's balcony. If an entire website can run on 2.5W, the question for our household isn't "do we have enough energy?" — it's "are we using it well?"

This philosophy applies directly to the renter energy challenge. The goal isn't to buy your way to net-zero with expensive products shipped from China. It's to apply creative engineering thinking — informed by both traditional low-tech solutions and modern knowledge — using locally sourced or salvaged materials wherever possible.

TheLastFarm on Substack reinforces this with a permaculture framing: "Permaculture seeks to create closed-loop systems. There is no 'waste': everything is the raw material for something else." The engineering projects below are attempts to close loops that industrial systems leave open.

## Problem 1: HVAC Reduction

*HVAC is 40-50% of a Southern home's energy bill. How do you dramatically reduce this as a renter?*

### Renter-Compatible Approaches (No Building Modification)

**Thermal curtains** on all windows (especially south and west-facing). Heavy, insulated curtains reduce heat gain 33% in summer and heat loss 25% in winter.

**Removable window insulation film.** Shrink-fit kits, ~$5-10 per window. Dramatic impact on heat loss in winter. Peel off in spring.

**Strategic fan placement.** Ceiling fans on summer setting reduce perceived temperature 4-6°F. A box fan in a window drawing cool night air through the house (when overnight lows drop below 75°F, which happens in Atlanta spring/fall) can eliminate AC need for months of the year.

**Portable evaporative coolers.** Less effective in Atlanta's humidity, but useful in shoulder seasons when humidity is lower.

**DIY thermal mass.** Large water containers (even 5-gallon buckets of water placed near sunny windows) absorb heat during the day and release it at night, moderating temperature swings. Low-tech Magazine documents how fiberglass water tanks are a simple way to moderate temperature swings with zero energy input. This is a passive "heat battery" with no moving parts.

**Textile layering.** Before the large-scale use of fossil fuels, removable textile layers kept homes warm in winter and cool in summer. Tapestries, heavy curtains, bed curtains, and layered clothing were the primary "HVAC" for millennia. Rediscover this. A heavy rug on a tile floor changes winter comfort dramatically. A canopy over the bed creates a microclimate you actually heat or cool, rather than conditioning the whole room.

### Landlord-Negotiation Approaches (Require Permission)

**Georgia Power EASE Program:** free attic insulation, air sealing, and duct sealing for qualifying customers (renters with landlord written consent). These three measures alone can reduce HVAC energy use 20-30%.

**Georgia Power HEIP:** 50% rebates on efficiency upgrades including HVAC servicing.

**Weatherstripping** doors and windows. Reversible, cheap, enormous impact.

**Attic insulation** is the single highest-ROI energy improvement in Southern homes.

## Problem 2: Significant HVAC Energy from Salvaged Materials

*Can you build a system that provides significant energy for HVAC using only locally sourced or salvaged materials?*

This is the "think big" engineering challenge. Several approaches worth investigating:

### DIY Solar Air Heater

A south-facing, wall-mounted panel (~3x6 feet) using a dark absorber surface behind clear glazing converts sunlight into hot air. Can be built from salvaged materials:
- Old window glass (Habitat ReStore, Craigslist free)
- Aluminum cans painted black (the classic beer-can solar heater)
- Plywood for the box
- Rigid foam insulation
- A small fan powered by a dedicated small PV panel

**Cost:** $50-200 in materials.
**Impact:** Can offset 30-70% of daytime heating during Atlanta's mild winters.
**Portability:** Completely removable when you leave.
**References:** Ecohome "How to Build a Solar Air Heating Panel"; OFF-GRID Daily Life "How to Build a DIY Solar Furnace" (Jan 2026); BuildItSolar.com.

The fan is key: passive convection works but produces modest heat. A small 5-10W fan powered by a dedicated PV panel increases output 3-5x, and the fan only runs when the sun is shining (exactly when you need it).

### Thermal Mass + Phase Change Storage

**Ice battery concept.** Using excess solar PV to make ice during peak sun, then using the phase-change energy for cooling later, is being explored by DIY builders in hot climates (Hackaday covered this in Mar 2026).

**Simpler renter version.** Freeze water containers during off-peak electricity hours (overnight, when rates are lower), then place them in front of a fan for passive cooling during the day. Low-tech but effective for supplemental cooling. The phase change from ice to water absorbs 334 J/g of energy — a significant cooling effect from a gallon of ice.

**Passive thermal mass.** Water has very high heat capacity. A 55-gallon drum of water in a sunny spot absorbs heat during the day and releases it slowly at night. Multiple 5-gallon containers along a south-facing wall accomplish the same thing with renter-appropriate modularity.

### Evaporative Cooling Tower

In Atlanta's shoulder seasons (spring, fall) when humidity is lower, a DIY evaporative cooler built from a 5-gallon bucket, cooling pads (or a wet towel), and a small fan can reduce room temperature 10-15°F for pennies of electricity. Less effective in peak summer humidity but meaningful for ~6 months of the year.

## Problem 3: Greywater Without City Approval

*Full plumbing-integrated greywater systems require permits and landlord cooperation in Georgia. What's actually possible without either?*

### Kitchen Greywater Reuse (Manual)
Save pasta water, rice water, vegetable washing water in a bucket. Use directly on garden plants. Zero infrastructure. Zero permits. Immediate impact. This alone can redirect 5-10 gallons/day from the sewer to the garden.

### Laundry to Landscape (Simplified)
If the washing machine drain hose is accessible and you use plant-safe detergent (no boron, no sodium), you can redirect the drain hose to an outdoor container during the wash cycle, then hand-carry to plants. This is manual and doesn't modify plumbing. A single load produces 15-30 gallons of greywater.

### Shower Greywater Capture
A bucket in the shower catches warm-up water while waiting for hot water. 2-5 gallons per shower, redirected to plants. Over a year, this is thousands of gallons.

These manual approaches aren't glamorous, but they collectively redirect significant water from the sewer to the garden — closing a loop without touching any plumbing. Call it "bucket greywater" — the engineering is ancient.

**Safety note for households with young children:** Standing water in buckets is a drowning hazard for toddlers — approximately 25 US children drown in buckets each year. Keep greywater buckets covered, elevated, or emptied immediately after use; never leave a water-filled bucket unattended on the floor [*The Urban Homestead*, Ch. 6].

## Problem 4: Stand-Alone Solar for a Renter

*What can you realistically power with a portable solar setup?*

Low-tech Magazine runs an entire website on 1-2.5W from a balcony solar panel. This establishes the lower bound: meaningful computation is possible on tiny power budgets.

### Practical Renter Configuration

- 200-400W portable solar panel(s) on balcony/patio
- 1-2 kWh LiFePO4 battery bank (portable, safe chemistry, long cycle life)
- Small inverter for AC loads (300-1000W)

**What this powers:** all device charging (phones, laptops, tablets), LED lighting for several rooms, a small fan, a worm bin heating mat in winter, small kitchen appliances (blender, electric kettle), the heated table described below.

**What it cannot power:** central HVAC, electric range, clothes dryer, water heater.

**Cost:** $800-1500 total, reduces annually as technology matures.

The value isn't energy independence — it's energy literacy. Running a portion of your household on visible, tangible solar power changes your relationship to energy consumption. You see the sun-to-use pipeline directly. The battery level is a real constraint that informs when to run the blender.

## The Kotatsu / Heated Table

*Heat the person, not the room.*

A kotatsu is a Japanese low table with a heating element underneath and a heavy blanket draped over the top. You sit at the table with your legs under the blanket. Only the person and their immediate space is heated — not the 2,000 cubic feet of air in the room.

Low-tech Magazine has published build plans for a modern Western version. A simple implementation:
- Any low table
- A small electric heating element (150-300W vs. 1500-3000W for whole-room heaters)
- A heavy blanket or comforter to create the "tent"
- Cushions to sit on

**Energy use:** ~10-20% of whole-room heating for equivalent personal comfort.
**Portability:** 100%.
**Cultural note:** the kotatsu is a social object. Family and friends gather at it. It's a design that combines warmth with togetherness. Works beautifully for a young family.

## The Haybox / Wonderbox / Retained-Heat Cooker

*The cooking equivalent of the kotatsu.*

A profoundly simple technology: bring food to a boil on the stove, then transfer the pot to a heavily insulated container. The food continues cooking for hours using only the retained heat.

**Construction options:**
- A cardboard box stuffed with wool blankets, towels, or hay
- A cooler
- A purpose-built insulated bag
- A wooden box with foam insulation

**What to cook in it:** Anything that simmers. Soups, stews, beans, rice, grains, oatmeal, hard-boiled eggs. Bring to boil for 5-10 minutes, transfer to haybox, let cook for 2-8 hours unattended.

**Energy impact:** Reduces cooking energy 50-80%. A batch of beans that would simmer for 2 hours on the stove cooks in 10 minutes of stove time plus 6 hours of free retained-heat cooking.

**Zero cost to make.** Old blankets and a cardboard box from a moving day will do.

**Historical note.** This is ancient technology. Traditional hay-insulated cooking boxes were used across Europe and Asia for centuries. Low-tech Magazine has published guides for building modern versions, including solar-powered variants that heat a heat-storage element directly from a solar collector.

**Why it matters for this project:** the haybox is a perfect example of the "ancestral skill + modern knowledge" synthesis. Simple, effective, zero-cost, zero permissions required, portable, and it closes a loop (reduces energy consumption for cooking, which means less grid dependence, which means the portable solar system covers more of household needs).

## Local Sourcing of Materials

For all DIY energy projects, prioritize:

**Salvaged materials.** Habitat for Humanity ReStore (Atlanta locations), Craigslist free section, neighborhood salvage, Nextdoor free listings, buy-nothing groups. The energy to manufacture a sheet of glass or a piece of plywood has already been spent — reusing it doesn't require more.

**Natural materials.** Southern pine (abundant, local). Georgia red clay (for thermal mass projects — a brick of kiln-fired clay absorbs heat beautifully). Straw bales (for deep insulation in haybox cookers).

**Locally manufactured.** Check Georgia-based suppliers before ordering online. Local steel fabricators for any metal work. Local lumber yards for wood.

**Communal sharing.** If the cooperative ring invests in tools (solar panel crimpers, multimeters, insulation blowers), they're shared across 8-15 families (see 06-community.md). The ToolBank serves community organizations — if the cooperative ring formalizes as a community group, they may access ToolBank resources.
