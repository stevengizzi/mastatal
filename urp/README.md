# Urban Renter Permaculture Design — Atlanta, GA

> A whole-systems permaculture design for a family renting in urban Atlanta for 3-5 years, with young children, pursuing a transition toward self-sufficient, community-embedded living.

**Authors:** Steven & Jessie Gizzi
**Origin:** Permaculture Design Course, Rancho Mastatal, Costa Rica — April 2026
**Status:** Living document

---

## The Thesis

**The garden, the village, and the household are the same design problem.**

Permaculture's three ethics — earth care, people care, fair share — are usually taught as separate concerns. But in urban rental life with young children, they collapse into a single design challenge: how do you build durable yields (food, community, resilience, skills) when your relationship to place is temporary?

The answer: **design for capacity, not infrastructure.** The most portable permaculture outputs aren't plants or earthworks — they're skills, relationships, preserved food, seed libraries, fermentation cultures, and the design methodology itself. The garden you leave behind is a gift. The village you build travels with you in the form of practice.

## The Four Ethics

1. **Earth care** — tending the ecological systems we depend on
2. **People care** — meeting human needs through cooperation and mutual support
3. **Fair share** — distributing surplus back to earth and people
4. **Transition** — the ethic that governs the journey between where you are and where you want to be

The fourth ethic is this project's contribution. You don't need 200 acres to practice permaculture. You start where you are, with what you have, and design each move to bring you closer to a more integrated life. The renter who composts, grows herbs, shares meals with neighbors, and rides MARTA is practicing permaculture — at a different scale, at a different point in the journey.

## The Guiding Question

Given: urban rental in Atlanta, 3-5 year tenancy, two adults (one composer transitioning to tech, one partner), young children incoming, limited budget, strong desire to live closer to permaculture principles.

How do we design household systems that:
- Yield within the tenancy window
- Build transferable capacity
- Close consumption loops wherever possible
- Shrink our dependency on distant, opaque supply chains
- Strengthen local community and economy
- Work within rental constraints (no structural modifications)
- Are baby-compatible (safe, low-maintenance, tolerant of neglect)
- Honor both degrowth ethics and a tech-enabled modern lifestyle
