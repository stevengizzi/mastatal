# Book Notes: *The Urban Homestead*

## Bibliographic Info

- **Authors:** Kelly Coyne & Erik Knutzen
- **Title:** *The Urban Homestead: Your Guide to Self-Sufficient Living in the Heart of the City* (Expanded & Revised Edition)
- **Publisher:** Process Media, 2010 (Process Self-Reliance Series)
- **Source format for these notes:** EPUB (no embedded page numbers)
- **Blog / website:** homegrownevolution.com
- **Reading status:** partial / mined for URP-relevant content

**Citation convention used here:** Because the EPUB contains no embedded page numbers, citations reference chapter and section heading, e.g. *Ch. 2, "Self-Watering Container"*. This is stable across editions and more verifiable than invented page numbers.

## Relevance to this project

Coyne and Knutzen are writing from Echo Park, Los Angeles, on a single-family lot — urban, but with a yard, in a Mediterranean climate rather than Atlanta's humid subtropical zone. They are owners, not renters, and most projects presume yard access. Within those constraints, this is the single closest-match book in the URP bibliography: their default posture is *"you can do this now, without moving, without much money, without asking permission, using stuff pulled out of the trash."* They pre-date the URP's closed-loop framing and pre-date AI entirely, but they already enact both. The book's highest value is **concrete, cheap, specific project-level how-to** — exactly the texture that some URP topic files currently abstract away from.

What transfers cleanly to the renter / Atlanta context: every indoor and container practice, the self-watering container construction, three-tier mulching, the foraging methodology, the fermentation / preservation / culture chapters, the cleaning cupboard, bike and walking transport, the philosophical posture, and the bias toward act-first. What transfers with caveats: anything requiring yard access, plumbing modifications, permanent structures, or long-residence plantings. What doesn't transfer: most of the livestock chapter (Ch. 4) given rental constraints, and any section presuming desert/Mediterranean climate (which Coyne & Knutzen frequently flag as context-specific themselves).

## Chapter 1 — Start Your Own Farm

### The four urban growing strategies (Ch. 1, "Strategies For Growing Food")

Coyne & Knutzen taxonomize urban growing into four strategies:

1. Container gardening
2. Edible landscaping
3. Community gardening
4. Guerrilla / pirate gardening

This four-strategy framing overlays cleanly on the URP's Zone map as a tactical layer: containers dominate Zones 0–1, edible landscaping is the landlord-permission conversation for Zone 2, community garden is Zone 3, guerrilla is a Zone 4 tactic requiring zero ownership. Useful for planning conversations where "zones" feels abstract.

### Window gardens as gateway (Ch. 1, "A Window Garden")

"Herbs are your gateway plants to a farming addiction." Reliable indoor starter herbs: **chives, parsley, cilantro, thyme** (basil and rosemary prefer outdoors but can be coerced with part-time outdoor rotation). Supplement short winter days with a compact fluorescent bulb positioned close to the plants — no grow light purchase required.

**Sprouting garlic cloves** pointy-side-up in a pot, cut the greens like chives. A good winter Zone 0 crop when other herbs go dormant. Shoots last about a month per clove; keep planting new cloves to maintain.

**Monofilament in-window trellis.** Transparent fishing line strung between window frame points creates a near-invisible trellis for pole beans climbing a south-facing window — a "living curtain." Fully reversible on move-out. *Genuinely new to the URP knowledge base.*

### Edible landscaping tactics (Ch. 1, "First Steps Toward An Edible Landscape")

Tactical doctrine for renters: **start by surreptitiously tucking herbs, radishes, and green onions around whatever is already planted** — usually a spindly shrub or uninspired ground cover. Incremental visual change that doesn't read as "modification."

Edibles that slot into ornamental beds without anyone noticing:
- Swiss chard, red lettuce, purple cabbage (for color)
- Strawberries as border plants
- Nasturtium (edible leaves, flowers, seed pods)
- Basil
- Chive plants (purple pom-pom flowers)
- Italian parsley
- Pea plants
- Pole/runner beans up a fence
- "Patio" cherry tomatoes

Coyne & Knutzen report their own parkway vegetable garden (square raised beds in a public median strip) had "nothing but pleasant comments from our neighbors, and no interaction with the city, though technically what we have done is illegal." Two years of operation. Neighbors bring their kids over to see what vegetables look like growing. "Come to consider front-yard gardening a key to our own community involvement." **This is the strongest practical argument the book makes for starting before the landlord conversation.**

### Seed balls / earth dumplings (Ch. 1, "How To Make Seed Balls")

"The seed ball is the Molotov cocktail of the urban homesteader." Peanut-M&M-sized balls of seed and clay, lobbed onto ground you can't plant or tend. The clay coat protects seeds from predation and weather until rain softens it.

Origin: Masanobu Fukuoka, *The One Straw Revolution* — he called them *tsuchi dango* (earth dumplings). Used globally in re-greening projects.

**Critical warning: never throw seed balls into natural areas.** Only into disturbed urban space (vacant lots, medians, construction aftermath). Inside natural areas they become invasive-species vectors.

Good insect-attractor seed choices: mustard, fennel, dill, buckwheat, clover, coneflower, goldenrod, yarrow, ironweed, sunflower.

### The Seven Guiding Principles of Successful Urban Farming (Ch. 1, "The Practicalities Of Growing Food")

Condensed orientation checklist:
1. Grow only useful things
2. Region matters, a lot
3. Build your soil
4. Water deeply and less frequently
5. Work makes work
6. Failure is part of the game
7. Pay attention and keep notes

"Grow only useful things" operationalized: **"If you water it, you gotta be able to eat it."** Every plant earns its place by being edible, medicinal, forage for animals, pollinator-attractor, or mulch source. Rules out pure ornamentals.

### Perennial prioritization (Ch. 1, "Grow Edible Perennials")

"To work less, dedicate a part of your garden to plants that bear food without you having to think about it." Relevant for renter-transient but still useful:

- All fruit and nut trees (including dwarf/semi-dwarf; some citrus container-growable)
- Fruiting bushes: blackberry, raspberry, **blueberry** (container-friendly due to acidic-soil requirement), grapes, gooseberry, red currant
- Perennial veg: asparagus, strawberries, fennel, artichokes, Jerusalem artichokes, rhubarb, chives and other herbs
- Annuals that behave perennially: leafy crops (broccoli, collards, kale, lettuce) give second harvests if cut to leave roots intact; many herbs self-seed — Italian parsley, cilantro, dill, arugula "pop up wherever they want"
- Divide perennial clumps (chives) to multiply

### Pest control & bird infrastructure (Ch. 2, "Don't Forget Your Bird Friends")

"Birds eat a phenomenal amount of insects." A birdbath pulls in the insect-eating birds that handle pest control for free. **A hubcap on a post qualifies as a birdbath.** Elevated so cats can't reach the birds. No bird feeder needed if your yard is bug-rich — the goal is to keep the birds hunting, not reward them for skipping the work.

Other organic pest control: hand-picking (toss bugs in soapy water), hose-blasting aphids, flooding ant nests, aviary netting over new beds. Ladybug larvae (dark red/black "tiny alligator creatures") eat *more* aphids than adult ladybugs.

**Attractor plants for beneficial insects:** angelica, bee balm, buckwheat, calendula, carrot-left-to-flower, chervil, cilantro, clover, daisy, dill, evening primrose, fennel, goldenrod, lovage, mustard, parsley, Queen Anne's lace, rue, sunflower, sweet alyssum, thyme, valerian, yarrow.

### Weeds as crop (Ch. 1, "Cultivate A Positive Relationship With Weeds")

"Keep on top of your weeds by eating them for dinner." Intentional edible-weed bed as infrastructure, not failure mode.

## Chapter 2 — Essential Projects

### Essential Project 1: Compost bin from a lidded garbage can (Ch. 2, "Start A Compost Pile")

Easiest compost bin: plastic or galvanized lidded trash can with holes punched in the sides for ventilation. If sitting on soil, punch holes in the bottom so worms come up through the soil. If on concrete, leave bottom intact. If directly on ground, move the bin periodically — the spot beneath becomes nutrient-rich planting ground.

Green : brown ratio roughly 50/50. Brown includes dry leaves, wood chips, sawdust (not pressure-treated; not glossy magazine paper), shredded newspaper. Green includes kitchen scraps, weeds, manure from herbivores, lawn trimmings, **used coffee grounds** (technically "green" despite color). Cover kitchen scraps with brown layer or get a "giant maggot party."

Moisture target: wrung-out sponge. Too dry → water it or "take a whiz on the pile once in a while" (nitrogen and moisture in one). Too wet → add brown stuff and stir.

Do **not** compost: bones, meat, fish, oily food, dog or cat poop, dairy — won't reach safe-decomposition temperature in a home pile.

Turning the pile speeds decomposition but is not required. "Turn your pile exactly as often as it pleases you." The fastest composting system is a worm bin (three months).

**Alternative bin: stacked tires** — cut out sidewalls, stack, fill from bottom with dry leaves/straw then food scraps. Black rubber heats the pile. Stack adds as the pile grows. Good option when no ground is available.

### Essential Project 2: Two-bin plastic-tote worm bin (Ch. 2, "Vermiculture")

Two 8–10 gallon opaque rectangular plastic storage bins with lids (opaque because worms hate light). One lb of red worms (*Eisenia foetida*, ~1000 worms — the proper composting species; from bait shops, farmers' markets, or online from a source near you so the worms don't ship cross-country).

Construction:
- Drill ~20 ¼" drainage/worm-migration holes in the bottom of both bins
- Drill small ventilation holes near the top rim and along lower edges of both bins
- Drill ~20 ventilation holes in one lid; leave the other lid intact (drip catcher)
- Optional: line the bottom of each bin with screening to prevent compost falling through drainage holes
- Bedding: 4" of damp shredded newspaper/cardboard/letter paper/brown paper (no glossy), plus a handful or two of soil for grit
- Bedding consistency: wrung-out sponge; worms drown if too wet

Setup: lay the intact lid on the ground as a drip-catcher. Set bricks on top. Rest one bin on the bricks. Cover with the ventilated lid.

**Harvest mechanism (the clever part):** When the working bin is mostly castings, set the second bin directly on top of the compost inside the first bin. Fill the new bin with damp newspaper + soil + fresh food scraps. Worms migrate up through the drainage holes to the new food over one to two months. Remove the lower bin — pure castings. This is more specific than the "nested migration" approach deserves credit for; it eliminates the usual castings-separation labor entirely.

Troubleshooting:
- Worms escaping out the bottom: desperate — too wet, too dry, too hot, or starving
- Bad smells: too wet, add newspaper and drill more vent holes; stop feeding
- Fruit flies: keep food well buried; lay cardboard over the bedding

**Liquid catch ("worm tea"):** If liquid drips out, feed it to plants.

### Essential Project 3: Three tiers of mulching (Ch. 2, "Mulch Your Yard")

"No other tip, trick or technique will help your garden more."

**Regular mulch:** A 5+ inch layer of organic matter around plantings. Less than 5" is "just decorating." Free annual mulch source: fallen leaves from your place and your neighbors'. Retains moisture, regulates soil temperature, suppresses weeds, feeds the soil as it breaks down, shelters beneficial insects (including ground spiders — "the wolves of the garden").

**Sheet mulch:** Regular mulch *plus* a weed barrier underneath. Wet newspaper (4–6 sheets thick, non-glossy) or wet cardboard, or 100% cotton sheets/towels/throw rugs. Lay directly over the existing lawn or weeds. **5+ inches of organic matter on top.** Plant directly through by sweeping mulch aside, cutting an X through the barrier, tucking the plant into the soil beneath, and closing the barrier back around it. Kills a lawn with zero digging or tilling.

Caveat: in hot, dry climates newspaper/cardboard sometimes bake into a water-repellent shield; skip the barrier if mulch floats when you water. Atlanta likely doesn't hit this threshold but worth knowing.

**Lasagna mulch:** Sheet mulching + soil remediation. For ground where nothing grows. Layers from bottom up:
1. Mow/stomp existing weeds; don't pull
2. 50 lbs of high-nitrogen manure per 100 sq ft (rabbit, chicken, horse — **not bagged cow**, too salty)
3. Weed barrier (wet cardboard/newspaper, heavy overlaps)
4. 3"+ compost layer (any organic matter without seeds — aged sawdust, dry leaves, dried grass, chopped yard trimmings, dried seaweed, homemade compost, or a mix). This layer doesn't have to look pretty.
5. 3"+ aesthetic top dressing (single material: dried leaves, straw, bark, wood chips, pine needles — what the neighbors see). Renew annually.

Let rest **one full year** before planting. Build raised beds or SWCs on top in the meantime for growing.

Mulch caveats in vegetable beds:
- Straw is clean and attractive; keeps zucchini/strawberries off bare soil
- Mulching cools soil — watch tomatoes and other heat-lovers in cold springs; pull mulch aside if soil looks cold or moldy
- Slugs and snails like to hide under mulch. Hunt with flashlights at 10–11 PM after afternoon watering.

### Essential Project 4: Raised bed construction (Ch. 2, "Build A Raised Bed")

Size guidance:
- Maximum 4 ft wide (reachable from one side), or narrower if only accessible from one side
- Height 1–2 ft standard; up to waist-high for wheelchair access or no-bending preference
- Classic 4 × 8 ft derives from standard lumber sizes

Orientation: north-south axis so plants get fair light distribution. Minimum 6 hours direct sun.

Materials: avoid railroad ties (creosote, carcinogen). Cedar or redwood last; scrap pallets or found lumber work but rebuild when they rot. Broken concrete ("urbanite") makes excellent free-form walls.

**Lead contamination bonus:** raised beds sidestep urban soil contamination by bringing in clean soil from outside (which is the URP's current implicit reason to grow in containers/beds on Atlanta red clay).

Hoop-frame upgrade: screw short 1" PVC sections vertically inside the bed walls; insert 10-ft ½" PVC lengths as hoops for bird netting or cold-frame plastic sheeting. Covered-wagon style.

### Essential Project 5: DIY Self-Watering Container (Ch. 2, "How To Build A Self-Watering Container")

**This is the single most valuable project in the book for the URP.** A self-watering container (SWC) holds a water reservoir at the bottom, separated from soil above by a ventilated divider. Water wicks upward through a wicking chamber. A plastic cover reduces evaporation. Result: a week between fill-ups even in summer, no over- or under-watering.

Why this matters for Atlanta rental balcony/patio growing: container gardening's two dominant failure modes are drought stress (forgot to water in 95°F heat) and root rot (overcompensated). SWCs eliminate both.

Construction from two 5-gallon buckets:

**Materials:**
- Two food-grade 5-gallon plastic buckets (restaurants and donut shops discard these free — the Kikkoman soy sauce bucket example is mentioned; never source from construction sites since those may have held toxic materials)
- One 16 oz plastic drink cup or 32 oz plastic yogurt container (the wicking chamber)
- One bucket lid (optional; can substitute a plastic garbage bag)
- Plastic twist ties
- 17 inches of 1" diameter PVC pipe, copper tubing, or bamboo tube (the fill pipe)
- A bag of potting mix — **must be potting mix, not garden soil**; SWCs don't drain and dense soil suffocates roots

**Tools:** drill, keyhole saw or utility knife.

**Steps:**

1. Cut a hole in the center of one bucket's bottom sized to accept your wicking chamber (yogurt container or basket). The chamber hangs through this hole into the reservoir below. Make sure the chamber isn't so tall the two buckets can't nest.
2. Cut a second hole near the outside edge of the same bucket's bottom for the fill pipe.
3. Drill ¼" ventilation holes throughout the remaining bottom surface. "Swiss cheese but not carried away." Leave the second bucket intact.
4. Punch or drill ½" holes randomly around the sides (not the bottom) of the wicking chamber. Bottom solid so soil doesn't fall out.
5. Attach the wicking chamber to the bottom of the top bucket with plastic twist ties at 12, 3, 6, and 9 o'clock.
6. Cut the fill pipe to length (17" is about right). Cut the bottom end on a diagonal so water flows freely out.
7. Nest the drilled bucket (with wicking chamber suspended) into the intact bucket.
8. **Drill the overflow hole:** ¼" hole in the side of the *outer* (lower) bucket, positioned just below the bottom edge of the inner bucket. This keeps the inner bucket wicking water rather than sitting in water.
9. Insert the fill pipe through its hole. Pointy end down.
10. Fill with potting mix (moisten as you fill).
11. Plant dead center.
12. **Fertilizer application:** Make a shallow trench around the plant's perimeter. Sprinkle about 1 cup dry organic fertilizer. Lightly cover with soil. Do NOT work the fertilizer into the soil. SWCs are closed systems — excess fertilizer can't drain away, so everything goes at the top to migrate down gradually.
13. Cover with the lid (cut an X for the plant to poke through) or a plastic garbage bag secured around the rim. This is the evaporation cover.

**Never use fertilizer tea in an SWC** — closed system accumulates.

Larger SWCs from 8–10 gallon storage tubs are better for a small salad garden, strawberries, or a blueberry bush. Josh Mandel's PDFs online have those plans; linked from the book's website.

**Best SWC crops:** basil (thrives on steady moisture), Italian parsley, patio/basket cherry tomatoes (not full-size — deep-rooted tomatoes will find the reservoir and waterlog), Swiss chard, lettuce, strawberries, blueberries.

Commercial alternative: **Earthbox** at earthbox.com, ~$40 per unit. Kelly's camp. Erik's camp: scavenge the buckets.

### Seed-starting and transplanting (Ch. 2, "How To Start Seeds" / "How To Transplant")

Worth doing from seed: plants you pull up whole (radishes, carrots, green onions) and plants eaten as babies (lettuce). Worth buying as seedlings: anything you only need 2–3 of (tomatoes, herbs). Both positions argued legitimately in the "Kelly says / Erik says" dialog.

Seed-starting containers: empty six-pack trays (seedling, not beer), divided nursery trays, or peat pots. Avoid egg cartons — dry out too fast.

Transplanting practice: dig a hole, backfill with the **same soil you dug out**, not fancy imported soil. Different soils won't blend; the hole becomes "an underground pot" and roots stop growing at the boundary. **Build an irrigation basin** — a 3-inch berm of soil ringing the plant at about the diameter of its branches. Fill the basin with water and let it soak in slowly rather than having water run off. Mulch thickly.

### Fertilizer tea (Ch. 2, "Fertilizer Tea")

Organic fertilizer steeped in water for ~24 hours. 5-gallon bucket of water + one of:
- A shovelful of finished compost
- A couple of cups of worm castings
- A few cups of dry organic fertilizer per package instructions
- A shovelful of 6-months-old horse or chicken manure (**not fresh**; not bagged cow — too salty)
- A shovelful of fresh rabbit poop (**the only poop safe fresh**) + a spoonful of molasses to activate
- An armload of weeds/trimmings (comfrey, plantain, dandelion, parsley, chicory especially; no seeded weeds)
- A few ounces of liquid kelp extract or fish emulsion (no steeping needed)

Cover to keep flies / dogs / toddlers out.

Two universal-recommendation use cases:
- Dilute tea for seedlings at first true leaves
- Post-transplant (any seedling or mature plant) to mitigate transplant shock

**Do NOT use fertilizer tea in self-watering containers.**

### Bean teepee (Ch. 2, "How To Make A Bean Teepee")

"Architecture that feeds you — who could ask for more?"

Six or more 6-foot-minimum poles (bamboo, salvaged branches, PVC, lathe), lashed at the top with wire/twine, sunk 6+ inches into the ground in a circle. Leave a wide gap between two poles for a door. Circle size depends on pole length and intended use — kid-size play fort, adult reading nook, or several small teepees for visual impact. Three to four bean seeds at the base of each pole; runner or pole beans suited to your climate.

Pick beans regularly or plants stop producing. Leave some beans on the vine late in the season to mature as next year's seed.

**Exceptional baby-compatibility flag:** this is, by construction, a shaded child-scale hideout that also produces food. Runner beans often flower red and attract hummingbirds. Combines Zone 1/2 growing with sensory play-space design.

### Polycultural vegetable bed (Ch. 2, "How To Design A Polycultural Vegetable Bed")

From Hemenway's *Gaia's Garden*. Plant a dense, random mix of vegetables with staggered maturation rates. The faster-growing plants shade the slower; density eliminates weeds; the thick canopy is itself living mulch that keeps soil moist and cool. As you harvest, tuck fresh seedlings into the empty spaces — the bed stays continuously full.

Avoid space hogs (corn, melons) in this system. Stick with lettuces, greens, root vegetables, beans, tender herbs, edible flowers.

Sample spring-fall mix (inspired by Ianto Evans's bed in *Gaia's Garden*): lettuce mix, carrots, parsnips, beans, beets, radishes, green onions, kale, cabbage, Swiss chard. Plant all at once, densely. Kale is the last thing pulled in fall — survives snow.

"The fun of polyculture is the experimenting."

### Tater tires (Ch. 2, "How To Make Tater Tires")

Stacked tires force potato plants to produce extra-long taproots. Three or four tires per stack, seed potatoes (ordered or from known-disease-free source, not grocery store which may carry growth inhibitors or disease). Reports of ~20 lbs per stack.

Atlanta window: February through April (Southern climes start earlier than temperate ones). Soil temperature 60–70°F ideal; tuber production stops above 80°F. Black tires warm soil (push earlier planting); paint white if the stack overheats.

Method: plant seed potatoes 2" below surface in a single tire of loose soil. When sprouts grow taller than one more tire, add that tire and fill with more soil/compost/hay/leaves, leaving just the top set of leaves exposed. Repeat for three or four tires. Water consistently but moderately.

Harvest: stop watering after flowers die and vines yellow. Remove top tire, harvest those, leave the rest in place, OR "punk rock style, kick the whole stack over." Never wash potatoes until ready to eat. Don't leave in sun — green potatoes are toxic. Cure 1–2 weeks before eating, store cool and dark (not the fridge).

### Pest control philosophy (Ch. 2, "Controlling Insect Pests")

"Healthy Plants, Healthy Attitude." Loss to insects is part of organic growing. Pull plants that consistently suffer rather than nursemaiding "fussy prima donnas." Hand picking, hose-blasting, ant control to manage aphids (ants "husband" aphids for honeydew), bird and spider support, and aviary netting on new beds.

Aphid control: ants are the upstream problem. Flood ant nests with a hose repeatedly; they relocate. For more serious ant control, boric acid (roach powder) + bait (sugar syrup, jelly, peanut butter, grease) at 1 tsp per 1 cup bait. Poke single hole in jar lid, lay on side in garden. Keep away from kids and pets.

### Lead contamination guidance (Ch. 2, "Why You Might Test Your Soil")

Urban soil is presumptively suspect. Possible contamination sources: previous auto repair or storage on-site, roadside / alley runoff, paint chips (especially from pre-1978 housing stock), industrial history. **Lead is especially concerning for households feeding young children, who absorb lead much more readily than adults.**

Testing: ~$30 through county health department referral to a certified lab.

Findings by plant part:
- **Fruiting crops** (tomatoes, peppers, squash, corn, apples) — safe; lead doesn't readily move into fruit bodies
- **Root vegetables** — surface contamination only; scrub and peel thoroughly
- **Leafy greens** — highest uptake; **grow in containers or raised beds with imported soil**

Air-deposited lead on foliage: peel outer leaves, scrub everything. **Wash produce in 1% vinegar water to remove surface lead** (University of Minnesota Extension).

Mitigation: "adding a heavy concentration of organic matter — mulch or compost — in the soil has the effect of reducing lead availability. It interferes with plant uptake of lead." More mulch, more compost.

**This is renter-critical for older Atlanta neighborhoods with pre-1978 housing.**

### No-dig principle (Ch. 2, "Not Digging It")

"Nature does not till." Tilling releases a short nutrient surge from killed soil organisms, then leaves plants nutrient-poor and destroys fungal/worm symbioses that take years to rebuild. Instead: compost and mulch *on top of* soil, forever. The system blends itself from the top down.

Exception: heavily compacted, truly dead ground. Either dig and amend once, or sheet-mulch and wait a year.

Dependency: works for raised beds and dedicated beds with paths between. Soil must stay loose and untrampled — no walking on beds.

## Chapter 3 — Urban Foraging

### The Six Things To Know About Eating Wild (Ch. 3, "Feral Edibles")

1. **You need a live teacher**, not just a book. Plants vary hugely by growing conditions. Wild-food walks via local nature centers, community college extension, Sierra Club hikes. Search "wild food" + city name.
2. **Identify by scientific name, not common name.** There are about 100 "chickweeds"; only *Stellaria media* is the edible one.
3. **Start easy.** Learn a handful of species; watch them through a year; add one at a time. **First priority: learn your local poisonous plants** — especially poison ivy in all four seasons, including dead winter stalks which still cause rash.
4. **Harvest from unsprayed places.** Vacant lots > golf courses. City parks > roadsides. Never former gas stations.
5. **Gather respectfully.** Don't pull the whole plant unless you're harvesting the root. Harvest no more than ⅓ of a plant to allow return harvesting. Careful gathering also prevents accidentally pulling lookalikes.
6. **Trust your instincts.** Bitter, bad-smelling, stomach-turning — stop. "You are not starving. You have nothing to prove." When testing a new species, cook it, eat a bite or two, wait, then scale up.

### Atlanta-relevant feral edibles (Ch. 3, "Feral Edibles")

Drawing only from the book's list items that grow in Zone 7b/8a:

- **Kudzu** (*Pueraria lobata*): "The scourge of the South, growing as fast as one foot a day, is said to taste like kale. The roots are high in calories, and if ground into powder make a cornstarch substitute — arrowroot powder. Plentiful livestock fodder." Kudzu is everywhere in the Atlanta bioregion. **This is a direct gift: one of the South's most maligned invasives is a free food source.**
- **Amaranth / pigweed** (*Amaranthus retroflexus*): native to the Americas, drought and heat tolerant, leaves steam/boil like spinach, fall seed heads edible in hot cereals and baked goods.
- **Chickweed** (*Stellaria media*): an "excellent starter weed," mild, high in vitamin C, salad-ready.
- **Wild blackberry** (*Rubus fruticosus*): fast colonizer of waste ground including construction aftermath. **Warning:** lookalike for poison ivy patches — be careful.
- **Spearmint / peppermint** (*Mentha* spp.): home-foraged and dried mint tea exceeds supermarket mint tea. Hang upside down in bunches to dry, strip, jar, store in dark.
- **Nasturtium** (*Tropaeolum majus*): all parts edible; peppery leaves, bright flowers in salads, seed pods pickle into "poor man's capers."
- **Prickly pear cactus**: young pads (*nopalitos*) edible; fruits (*tunas*) taste like watermelon. Singe or skin to remove invisible prickers.

### Fruit foraging tools (Ch. 3, "Tools For The Fruit Harvester")

- **Fruit picker** — long-pole wire cage with claws at the opening; ~$20 at any garden center. Claws dislodge, basket catches.
- **Berry hook** (from Samuel Thayer, *Forager's Harvest*): stick with a hook at one end, rope at the other. Hook a flexible branch, draw it down, stand on the rope tail to keep both hands free to pick. **DIY from a broomstick and a large hook screw.** Doubles as a dumpster-diving retrieval tool.
- **Collecting bin:** shoulder bag (old messenger bag) or small tub tied around the waist. Keeps both hands free.

### Fallen Fruit manifesto (Ch. 3, "Fruit Foraging")

Fallen Fruit (fallenfruit.org) — LA-based activist art collective that maps urban fruit trees on public and parkway land and advocates that cities only plant fruit-bearing trees in public parks. Direct conceptual ancestor of Atlanta's **Concrete Jungle** and their Fruit Tree Map of 4,200+ trees. Worth naming this lineage.

Legal note: "The law states that any fruit growing in a parkway strip, or on branches hanging over a sidewalk or alley is in public space and therefore fair game for you to pick." Ask permission for owner trees, but parkway and overhang fruit is public.

### Acorn processing (Ch. 3, "How To Eat Acorns")

Acorns were a Native American staple. Rich in calories, complex carbs, fats, some protein. Tannin makes them bitter and, in sufficient quantity, can cause kidney damage — but tannin is so bitter you can't eat enough to hurt yourself. Process to remove.

**White oaks (rounded leaves) are preferred** over red oaks (pointy-lobed leaves) — larger, sweeter nuts. Taste raw acorns from candidate trees; choose the least bitter.

Harvest September. Float test: discard any that float (bugs). Pop off the hats, peel the soft shells with pliers. Discard any with dark or dusty interiors.

Grinding: blender with water makes a creamy mash. (Traditional: two rocks.)

**Leaching:** Line a colander with cheesecloth or a dishtowel. Pour in mash. Tie up the corners into a bundle. Submerge bundle in a bowl of water. Change water when it goes cloudy. Several changes over a day (can sit overnight between). Done when the water stays clear and bitter mouth-pucker is gone. **Do not boil** — kills nutrients.

Squeeze out excess water. Use mash fresh, freeze, or dry on cookie sheets at low heat into acorn meal.

Uses: substitute or blend with cornmeal in cornbread, cornmuffins, pancakes. Porridge (simmer like oatmeal). Thickener for stews.

Acorn meal keeps poorly — refrigerate, use soon.

**Atlanta relevance:** Atlanta's "city in a forest" has abundant white and red oaks. This is a significant free-calorie annual fall harvest currently absent from the URP.

### Dumpster diving (Ch. 3, "Dumpster Diving")

Legal status: **not illegal except where specifically prohibited.** *California v. Greenwood* (1988) — no common-law expectation of privacy on discarded materials. Ignore signs and locks; never break either; never jump fences.

Etiquette: building owners fear *dumping* more than *diving.* Don't strew trash. Be tidy.

High-yield targets:
- **Bakeries** — day-old bread, pastries, bagels daily
- **Markets** — crate-quantity bruised / just-expired produce and dairy; leverages home preservation skills enormously (box of bruised apples → fruit butter; soft tomatoes → sauce; milk at expiry → yogurt)
- **Nurseries** — ripped bags of soil/compost/mulch they can't sell
- **Candy stores** — post-holiday (e.g., February 15th)
- Any discontinued product line

Reference: John Hoffman, *The Art and Science of Dumpster Diving*. Coyne & Knutzen call it persuasive enough to "change the way you think forever."

### Day-old bread revival (Ch. 3, "Revive Day-Old Bread")

Mist with water (or dip under running tap for 1 second, or spray pastries to preserve toppings). 350°F oven, 5 min for small things, 10 min for big loaves.

Uses for revived or stale bread: croutons, French toast, pain perdu, bread pudding (decadent with croissants), toasted bread crumbs (spiced, as a Parmesan substitute on pasta and vegetables), bruschetta, **panzanella** (Italian bread-and-tomato salad — stale bread cubes + chopped ripe tomatoes + onion + parsley + basil + 1:3 vinegar:olive oil dressing + salt & pepper; rest 10 min).

## Chapter 5 — Revolutionary Home Economics

### Framing: Preservation is transformation (Ch. 5, "Preservation Is Transformation")

"Culturing, drying and most of all fermenting foods opens up whole worlds of flavor and texture that just do not come about any other way." Canning preserves — keeps food *unchanged*. Culturing and fermenting *transform* — create something genuinely different, with its own flavor and benefits. Frames the URP's "perpetual cultures" loop in philosophical terms.

**Hygiene hypothesis sidebar:** Pasteurization, canning, freezing, and irradiation protect against bad bugs but kill all the good ones. Modern sterile environments may be causally linked to allergies, asthma, autoimmune disorders. Cultured/fermented foods reintroduce beneficial microbial exposure. *This is a values-laden argument but aligns with the URP posture.*

Two cited books they cannot recommend enough:
- Gardeners and Farmers of Terre Vivant, *Preserving Food Without Freezing or Canning*
- **Sandor Ellix Katz, *Wild Fermentation*** — "as much a manifesto against lifeless food as it is a cookbook." *Worth flagging for potential future mining.*

### Sterilization discipline (Ch. 5, "First Things First")

- Sterilize jars (10 min boil, covered, with rack or towel at bottom) for anything NOT heat-processed: yogurt, flavored vinegars, lacto-fermented pickles
- For canning: quart jars don't need separate sterilization (canning sterilizes them); **pints and half-pints do need sterilization** (shorter process times don't reach sterilization)
- **Never boil canning lids before use** — the sealing compound is single-use and heat-activated
- **Never reuse canning lids.** Rings and jars are reusable forever

### Water-bath canning basics (Ch. 5, "How To Can")

For high-acid foods (fruit, tomatoes with added acid, pickles). Not for low-acid vegetables, meat, or soup — those require a pressure canner (outside the book's scope).

Equipment: canner or big stock pot with rack or folded towel at bottom, canning tongs, bendy spatula / chopstick, funnel (optional but helpful). Canning kit ~$50 online if hardware stores don't stock.

Process:
- Jars and contents both **hot** at packing time. Never pack cooled contents into hot jars.
- Pack one jar at a time, finger-tight lid, ¼" headspace, air-pocket-removal with chopstick.
- Submerge in boiling water by 2 inches. Process time varies (10–50 min typical; charts in canning manuals / online).
- Remove to towel on counter. **Don't fiddle.** Listen for the "ping" of lid sealing.
- After 24 hours: press lid center. Should be slightly concave and not pop up and down. If it does, use the jar immediately or reprocess.
- Store dark. Eat within a year.

**Botulism honesty:** Fewer than 200 cases/year in the US, most from bad home canning. "If there is any doubt about something you've canned, just cook it before you eat it. Cooking at high heat for 10 minutes kills *C. botulinum*."

### Lacto-fermentation (Ch. 5, "Pickling via Lacto-Fermentation")

Pre-canning pickling. Salt + vegetable. Lactic bacteria colonize, acidify the brine, suppress spoilage bacteria. Keeps without canning or refrigeration (though both extend shelf life).

Universal rule: **never use iodized salt** — iodine kills lactobacillus.

**Daikon radish pickles (beginner recipe):**
- Daikon scrubbed, peeled, cut into rounds/quarters/matchsticks ¼–⅓" thick (not long spears — they tend to poke above brine line as the jar empties)
- 1 peeled garlic clove (optional), a few peppercorns
- 2+ tablespoons non-iodized sea salt
- 1 quart water (filtered or tap)

Mix salt and water separately. Pack daikon in a very clean quart jar. Pour brine over, leave ¼" headspace. Close tight. Store in cool dark cabinet.

Try at 3–4 days; we (the authors) typically wait a week. Some fizzing when opened is normal. Should be crunchy, transformed, garlic-flavored. Fridge extends life.

Same technique applied to: baby cucumbers with dill and garlic, parboiled pearl onions, sliced turnips, nasturtium seed pods.

Long-term storage: counter (weeks, shorter in summer), fridge (long), or can them (kills live cultures; defeats the health point but safe for multi-year storage).

**L'hamd markad (preserved salted lemons, Ch. 5):** Moroccan application. Lemons (thin-skinned Meyers preferred), sea salt "tons of it," fresh lemon juice, optional spices (cinnamon, cloves, peppercorns). Quarter lemons, coat in salt, pack and press to release juice, layer with more salt. Cover with lemon juice. Dark cupboard 4–6 weeks. Rinds become soft. Use chopped as flavor punch — like olives or anchovies — in salads, fish, hummus. Fridge after opening, keeps 6 months.

**Euell Gibbons crock:** 5-gallon food-grade plastic bucket or ceramic crock for bulk batches. Plate on top to keep vegetables submerged. Cheesecloth cover. Mold on top is not catastrophic — skim off.

### Dehydration (Ch. 5, "Dehydration")

Removes 80%+ of moisture from fruits, 90%+ from vegetables. Target temperature: 100–140°F (above 180° you're cooking; below 100° contamination risk). Doneness test: leathery, no moisture when squeezed.

Ascorbic acid dip (2 tsp vitamin C powder per quart water, add 1 tsp per third batch) prevents browning without sulfites.

**Post-drying storage:** airtight containers, tight lids. Pantry moth story about sun-dried tomatoes turning into "a jar full of seething larvae." Freezing for a few days post-drying pasteurizes, but kills some flavor; used only for moth-prone items like figs.

Three methods:

1. **Sun-drying:** Works only in desert conditions — even moderate humidity prevents it. Atlanta is not a candidate.
2. **Air-drying herbs:** No low-humidity requirement. Bundle and tie by stems, hang upside down **indoors, out of direct sunlight.** Ready in a few days when leaves crumble at touch. Strip leaves, jar, store dark. Works fine in Atlanta.
3. **Appalachian Solar Food Dryer:** Homemade two-stage solar dehydrator. Works in most climates, including humid ones. Long shallow glass- or plastic-covered collection box with black interior + cabinet with racks above it. Convective airflow pulls heat up into the drying chamber. "Takes about a day and a half to dry tomatoes." Plans: *Home Power Magazine* issue 57 (Feb/Mar 1997); refinements in issue 69 (Feb/Mar 1999); PDF downloads at homepower.com (~$5). Eben Fodor's book *The Solar Food Dryer* has a half-size version and a one-box design. A quick-and-cheap cardboard-box variant works for one summer.

**Atlanta relevance:** the Appalachian dryer is the cooking-side companion to the haybox already in 03-engineering-projects.md. Both substitute sunlight / retained heat for grid electricity.

### Herb-infused vinegar (Ch. 5, "Preserving With Vinegar")

Glass bottle, no metal caps (corrosion). Sterilize bottle. Clean, dry herbs; picked in morning after dew dries (their peak). Good candidates: dill, parsley, rosemary, thyme, tarragon, sage, peppercorns, berries.

Matching: plain white vinegar with delicate herbs; apple cider with fruit; wine vinegar with strong herbs.

Ratio: roughly 1 part herb to 4 parts vinegar, loose.

Heat vinegar nearly to boil. Pour over herbs. Seal. Steep in dark cabinet 3–4 weeks. Strain through cheesecloth or coffee filter into fresh sterilized bottle. Keeps 6–8 months fridge, 3 months cabinet.

### Yogurt making (Ch. 5, "Making Yogurt")

- 1 quart milk (whole = creamiest)
- 1 tablespoon **live-culture** yogurt (friend, store container labeled "contains live cultures")
- Quart jar, small insulated cooler, thermometer

Heat milk to 180°F (double boiler or thick-bottom pot; stir; don't boil). Cool to 110°F (touchable, like hot bath). Preheat jar and cooler with hot water while waiting.

Stir 1 tablespoon yogurt into milk — **just one**, more isn't better. Pour cultured milk into hot jar, cap, place hot jar in hot cooler. Fill cooler with 110°F water (hot water bottles help). 8–12 hours. Check water temp; add fresh hot water as needed.

Should be thick and tangy. If not, add another spoon of starter, re-heat the cooler, wait again.

Save some of the last batch to culture the next. As long as not moldy, cultures are viable. Yogurt gets more sour as it ages in the fridge.

**Labaneh (yogurt cheese):** drain yogurt through cheesecloth-lined strainer into bowl. Overnight or workday gives cream-cheese texture, tangy. 2 cups yogurt → 1 cup cheese. Flavor with herbs or fruit. Classic sandwich: on pita, studded with olives, thyme, mint, tomato slices. Drained whey is protein-rich; add to smoothies or substitute for water in recipes.

**Fil / piima / viila (Scandinavian mesophilic cultures):** Perpetual milk culture that **needs no heating.** 2 tsp of last batch into a quart of fresh milk or cream, leave on counter 24 hours. Refrigerate when thickened to taste. In milk: texture between buttermilk and yogurt, clean-tasting, faint lemon sourness. Drink plain, bake with, blend with fruit for smoothies, salt + cucumber + mint for *lassi*-like cold drink. **In cream:** transforms into a *crème fraîche* / clotted cream / sour cream / whipped cream analog. "Good enough to eat straight off the spoon."

Sourcing cultures: friend, or G.E.M. Cultures by mail.

**This is the URP's "perpetual cultures" loop made concrete at higher resolution than sourdough alone.** A household that keeps sourdough + fil + kombucha SCOBY has fully closed the bread / dairy-culture / fermented-drink loops with three living cultures that reproduce indefinitely.

### Fruit butter, jams, and the five-tomato protocol (Ch. 5)

**Fruit butter (e.g., apple butter):** a slow reduction. Cook down fruit with sugar and spices until thick and dark. No pectin needed. Canning-friendly.

**Five Ways to Preserve a Tomato (Ch. 5):**

1. **Extend the harvest.** Unripe tomatoes at first frost: wrap each in newspaper, stack ripest-on-top in a box; *or* pull up the whole plant and hang upside down. Ripen over weeks at cool-but-not-freezing temp. Green tomatoes at a loss: fried green tomatoes, jam, chutney; squeeze lemon, treat like bell peppers.
2. **Dried tomatoes** — best with cherry or Roma/paste varieties. Drying intensifies sweetness into "tomato candy."
3. **Candied tomatoes for the freezer** — oven-baked in olive oil. 2–2.5 lbs tomatoes cored and halved (or wedged), 1 cup olive oil, salt. Arrange cut-side down ½–1" apart in metal pan. 400° 30 min → 350° 30 min → 300° 30 min (then 250° 10–15 min if edges not colored). Cool 20 min. Layer in containers, cover with oil, refrigerate or freeze up to 3 months. Adapted from Lynne Rossetto Kasper, *The Italian Country Table*.
4. **Home-canned whole tomatoes** — 2.5–3.5 lbs per quart. Blanch and peel. **Add bottled lemon juice: 2 tbsp per quart, 1 tbsp per pint** (necessary acid for water-bath safety). Pack hot into hot jars, ½" headspace. Quart: 45 min boil. Pint: 40 min.
5. **Canned tomato sauce.** Simple reduction: cover bottom of stock pot with olive oil; sauté garlic (~5 cloves per 3 lbs tomatoes) till soft; add tomatoes (cored, peeling optional); bring to lively bubble; reduce by half; salt and pepper; puree with immersion blender / countertop blender / food mill. Acidify with bottled lemon juice or citric acid per canning guide. Freeze in ice cube trays → freezer bags if preferred. "If the sauce tastes a little off as a result, counterbalance the lemon by adding a bit of sugar."

### Sourdough (Ch. 5, "Baking With Sourdough")

Why wild yeast beats commercial: "richer in certain vitamins and enzymes that are by-products of lactic fermentation, and it contains less phytic acid, which blocks mineral absorption." (Citing Laurence Kaplan, *Good Bread is Back*.) Keeps longer than commercial-yeast bread.

**Starting the starter:**
1. Glass or ceramic container with lid, 3+ cups capacity (quart Mason jar works). Not metal.
2. Begin with white flour (whole wheat and rye molds before cultures establish). Convert later.
3. Day one: 1 cup white flour + 1 cup lukewarm water. Stir smooth. Warm place (70–80°F ideal; top of stove in winter, counter in summer).
4. Every day: **pour off 1 cup**; add ½ cup flour + ½ cup lukewarm water. Feed every single day without fail — the most common failure mode is skipped feedings.
5. Bubbles appear in a few days. "Hooch" (liquid layer on top) is normal; stir in.
6. Usable after 2 weeks. After 1 week, try a loaf (or sourdough pancakes with the poured-off cup).
7. Fridge storage: feed weekly. Revive with 2–3 days of daily feedings before baking.
8. Convert to whole-wheat or rye: after 2 weeks of a healthy white starter, switch feed flour to whole wheat or rye. Converts in a few days. **Feed whole wheat and rye starters daily without exception** — they mold faster.

**Debunked sourdough myths (Ch. 5, "Sourdough Mythologies"):**
- Adding grapes/potatoes/rice does not help — wild yeasts are in the flour, not on grape skins (those are wine yeasts).
- Adding commercial yeast is pointless — it dies in the acidic environment of a healthy starter.
- Mail-ordered starters: local wild yeasts take over within weeks regardless. Just make your own.
- Bottled water: plain chlorinated tap works. If heavily chlorinated and you have trouble, leave water uncovered 24 hours to dechlorinate.
- "Catching yeast from the air": yeasts are overwhelmingly in the flour.

**Sourdough recipe (adapted from Nancy Silverton, *Breads from the LaBrea Bakery*):**
- 8 oz starter
- 13 oz unbleached white bread flour
- 3 oz whole wheat flour
- 2 tbsp wheat bran
- 8 oz cool water
- 1.5 tsp sea salt

By weight, not volume (humidity makes flour-by-cup unreliable). Cheap scale fine.

Mix starter, flours, bran, water to a sticky dough. Knead 5 min (hand or mixer). Rest 20 min under cloth. Mix in salt and knead another 6 min. Oiled bowl, cover, warm ferment 3–4 hours. Shape into a *boule* (flat ball), place in floured proofing basket (banneton) or floured-cloth-lined bowl. **Refrigerate 8–24 hours** — long cold rest develops flavor. Out of fridge, ferment another 3–4 hours warm.

Preheat oven to 500°F with baking stone inside. Turn boule onto flour/cornmeal-covered cardboard. Slide onto stone. **Immediately drop to 450°F and spritz oven walls with water** (or toss a shot glass of water against the wall) — steam injection for hard crust. "The revolution will not be soft-crusted." Spray again 2–3 times over the next 5 min. Bake total 40–45 min. Rotate at 20 min. Dark brown crust = done. **Cool fully before slicing** (still cooking inside; active yeasts can cause stomach upset).

### Cleaning cupboard — The Big Three (Ch. 5, "Our Cleaning Cupboard")

**Three principles of clean:**
1. Nontoxic, easy on skin (no gloves required)
2. Cheap
3. Multi-purpose (few items under the sink)

**Baking soda** — scrubber, deodorizer, laundry additive (softens water, improves soap performance), toothpaste ingredient. Buy in bulk (restaurant supply or big-box).

**Distilled white vinegar** — all-purpose cleaner, mineral deposit remover, mildew spray, fruit/veg wash, laundry additive. Dilute 50/50 with water in a spray bottle as an all-purpose spray.

**Liquid castile soap** (e.g., Dr. Bronner's) — dish soap, hand soap, body soap, laundry, floor cleaner. Dilute heavily.

**Toxicity warning:** Commercial cleaning products do **not** require independent safety testing before sale. Under the Toxic Substances Control Act, burden is on EPA to prove harm, not on manufacturer to prove safety. ~2,000 new chemicals submitted annually; most approved in ~3 weeks. Many household cleaning chemicals linked in the research literature to cancer, asthma, allergies, immune disorders, reproductive disorders.

**"Nuclear option" disinfection:** Spray suspect surface with straight hydrogen peroxide, then straight white vinegar (either order, separate bottles — **never mix in one bottle**, creates peracetic acid). Developed by Susan Sumner at Virginia Polytechnic Institute; tested on heavily contaminated surfaces; eliminates virtually all salmonella, shigella, and E. coli. Nontoxic; rinses with water; leaves no flavor.

**Produce wash:** Running water is adequate per FDA. Commercial washes show no better performance. Homemade soap/vinegar/bleach washes leave residue that affects flavor. *Exception: 1% vinegar water for lead-contaminated urban soils* (cited earlier from Ch. 2).

**Drain maintenance:** Cup of baking soda + 2 cups boiling water or 1 cup vinegar periodically. For actual clogs: hair catcher prevents; clogs already formed require a **crank auger** (sink/tub) or **closet auger** (toilet). Toilet auger has a protective tube so the snake doesn't chip porcelain. Not interchangeable.

**Kitchen rag discipline:** Cotton dishcloths rotated every couple of days beat cellulose sponges for bacterial control. Downgrade aged cloths to cleaning rags; compost at end of life if all-cotton.

**Toilet cleaning:** 50/50 vinegar-water on seat and bowl. For inside: bucket-flush to lower water level, pour straight vinegar around sides, scrub. Escalation: straight baking soda scrub. Foaming when the two meet is harmless (unlike the dreaded bleach/ammonia reaction).

**Magic soft scrub:** ½ cup baking soda + castile soap to frosting consistency, few drops essential oil. Scrubs tub rings, soap scum, gunky sinks. Rinse after.

## Chapter 6 — Be Your Own Utility: Water And Power

### Water principles for the urban homesteader (Ch. 6, "Water Principles")

Three-principle frame:
1. **Rain is a resource, not a problem.**
2. **Water can be recycled.** (Greywater rather than sewer.)
3. **Water-saving strategies should be simple and cheap.** "Putting in a greywater system that costs thousands of dollars and requires constant maintenance makes no sense from an economic or environmental perspective. Is our system up to code? No. Is it functional, ecologically sound and inexpensive? Yes."

Prioritization sequence for irrigation: rainwater first → greywater second → municipal only when both fail.

### Water use breakdown (Ch. 6, "Where Does All That Water Go?")

Typical American home:
- Landscaping: 58.7%
- Toilet: 10.8%
- Clothes washer: 8.7%
- Shower: 6.8%
- Faucet: 6.3%
- Hidden leaks: 5.5%
- Dishwasher: 0.6%

The dominant share is outdoor — which is why rain barrels and greywater on outdoor plants is the highest-leverage single intervention.

Per-capita context: average American uses 80–100 gallons/day. Average African uses 5 gallons/day. Americans consume 7% of the world's total energy pumping and treating water (Center for Sustainable Systems).

### Six rainwater harvesting techniques (Ch. 6)

Brad Lancaster framework (*Rainwater Harvesting for Drylands*): work from the highest point in your yard — roof — to the lowest. Observe where water already flows in a storm.

**Technique 1: Depaving.** Sledgehammer (or rented jackhammer for dense concrete; rented concrete saw for clean cuts). Replace impermeable surfaces with wood chips, unmortared brick, decomposed granite, or plants. Driveways only need two tracks of stone or brick under the wheels — rest can be permeable. Broken concrete ("urbanite") makes excellent raised beds and retaining walls. Leave concrete against foundations to prevent water intrusion. Atlanta renter applicability: **depends on landlord; frame as "improves drainage."** Renter-possible in specific cases (alley corners, unused back patio edges).

**Technique 2: Smart gutters and downspouts.** Multiple downspouts distribute load. Downspout-to-drainage-pipe (4" white plastic) takes water out to mulch basins. Code requires downspouts carry water at least 10 ft from structure. **Annual gutter cleaning** is non-negotiable — ½" hardware cloth strainer baskets in each downspout prevent clogs.

**Technique 3: Earthworks.** Berms (mounds) and swales (depressions) that channel and hold water near plant roots. Shovel is the tool. Observe existing water paths, then shape soil to direct flow into planting zones. Free tactic; highest land-use yield per dollar.

**Technique 4: Mulch basins.** Shallow trench with raised walls, filled with wood chips. Holds runoff from downspout or greywater outlet until it sinks. Mulch prevents stink, evaporation, and mosquito breeding. Chain basins on sloped land so overflow cascades. **Great location for trees.** Sizing: trial and error; clay soils need wider/shallower; upside-down bucket with holes inside the basin improves infiltration in heavy clay. **Atlanta red clay relevance: clay soils absolutely need the bucket modification.**

**Technique 5: Terracing.** Hillsides cascade water away before it absorbs. Terrace with retaining walls (stone, broken concrete, wood). Creates a "lens" of water that migrates down through the slope over days. Plant drought-tolerant at top, water-needy at bottom.

**Technique 6: Rain barrels and cisterns.** (Already in URP.) Two noteworthy additions:
- **IBCs (Intermediate Bulk Containers):** 275 or 330 gallon rectangular plastic-in-metal-cage tanks. Space-efficient (fit within a shipping pallet footprint). $140–250 new or reconditioned. Already have a drain at the bottom — no bulkhead modification needed. Stackable. "A good option for folks who don't want to tinker."
- **Metal roof tip:** If the landlord ever replaces the roof, advocate metal. "Unlike asphalt shingle, doesn't shed undesirable stuff into your water. Metal roofs will last a lifetime and also reflect the heat of the sun, keeping your house cooler in the summer."

### Atlanta roof-water calculation (Ch. 6, "How Much Water Can I Harvest From My Roof?")

The book includes a worked Atlanta example: *"If we transported our house to Atlanta, where the average annual precipitation is 48.6 inches per year (except in recent drought years!) we could theoretically collect 26,034 gallons of water a year"* — from a 992 sq ft asphalt shingle roof.

Formula: Roof area (sq ft) × efficiency factor (asphalt shingle: ~0.6; metal ~0.9+) × 0.9 × annual rainfall in inches = gallons/year.

**This is a direct Atlanta-specific number the URP can cite verbatim.** The 26,034 gal/year figure is sobering against the typical ~1,000 gal/month household usage — the roof alone could theoretically cover nearly all outdoor irrigation needs and then some.

Sizing guidance: you don't need a tank sized for the whole year. Calculate monthly rainfall and size to bridge dry gaps between rainstorms.

### Greywater (Ch. 6)

Reference: Art Ludwig, *Create an Oasis with Greywater* (oasisdesign.net). "The most informative source on greywater."

**Safety claim from the book:** "Nobody in the US has ever gotten sick from exposure to greywater." (Per Ludwig.) Plumbing codes are overly cautious; "the Man quite simply wants you to throw perfectly good water down the sewer."

**Six greywater safety rules:**
1. Never on crops eaten raw (strawberries, carrots, lettuce). Fine on fruit trees where fruit is above the ground.
2. Never onto lawns or directly onto plant foliage — microorganism party. "Purified by moving through soil."
3. Never via sprinkler — don't aerosolize potential pathogens.
4. Not from washing machine when washing diapers.
5. Don't let greywater stand — anaerobic bacteria, mosquitoes, stink. Use immediately.
6. Only plant-compatible soaps and detergents.

**Plants that tolerate greywater (alkaline-tolerant):** bananas, citrus, blackberries, elderberries, currants, loquat, pineapple, guava, and most fruit and nut trees. **Avoid greywater for:** blueberries (acid lovers), ferns, shady forest dwellers. Direct conflict note for URP: **blueberries are a core URP Zone 2 crop and must be excluded from any greywater scheme.**

**Unsafe detergent ingredients** (California DWR, *Greywater Guide*):
- Chlorine / chlorine bleach
- Peroxygen
- Sodium perborate
- Sodium trypochlorite
- Boron
- **Borax** — common homemade-detergent ingredient is NOT greywater-compatible
- Petroleum distillate
- Alkylbenzene
- "Whiteners"
- "Softeners"
- "Enzymatic" components

**Baking soda and washing soda also kill plants** (salt buildup). Powdered soaps and bar soaps also avoid.

**Phosphates are fine for greywater** (beneficial to soil). The "no-phosphate" label is a lake / ocean concern, not a greywater concern. Counterintuitive.

Switch bar soap to liquid soap in the shower. Most shampoos are compatible. Recommended laundry detergent: **Oasis Biocompatible** (bio-pac.com).

Occasional fresh-water irrigation to flush salt buildup.

**Non-invasive greywater methodologies (Ch. 6):**
- **Bucket in the shower** for warm-up water (technically not greywater yet). Houseplants, outdoor plants, toilet-flushing.
- **Bathtub siphon out the window** — siphon from auto parts store. Drain the tub to the garden.
- **Bathroom sink disconnection** — let drain into a 5-gallon bucket under the sink. Use for plants or manual toilet flushing. **Critical safety note: "Around 25 kids drown in buckets every year in the US, so don't leave buckets unattended if you have little ones."** This is directly relevant to the URP's baby-safe constraint.

### Energy breakdown (Ch. 6)

The book's rough split of household energy by end use:
- Heating: 49%
- Cooling: 49%
- Water heater: 13%
- Lighting: 10%
- Stoves + small appliances: 8%
- Electronics: 7%
- Washer + dryer: 6%
- Refrigerator: 5%
- Dishwasher: 2%

The heating+cooling percentages don't sum to 100 because they're climate-alternating dominants — one or the other is the main load depending on region. In Atlanta's cooling-dominated climate, cooling is the primary lever. **This supports and refines the URP's current "HVAC 40–50%" claim** — the real load is closer to ~50% cooling specifically, and every other household intervention is a rounding error against cooling efficiency.

**Parabolic cooker** and built-in **wall cooker** — solar cooking devices, conceptually adjacent to the URP's haybox. Not explored in depth here but the chapter mentions both. *Possible future extraction target if solar cooking becomes a project.*

## Chapter 7 — Transportation

### Transportation principles (Ch. 7)

Four principles, all of which align with URP framing already:
1. **The journey is the journey** — commuting can be joyful, not extracted time.
2. **Transportation should be low-tech** — you should be able to repair it yourself.
3. **Transportation should give you exercise.**
4. **Transportation should promote community** — "Traffic circulation is the organization of universal isolation" (Guy Debord).

### Walking (Ch. 7, "Walking")

"Draw a circle on a map around your homestead. Everything within a mile is an easy walk. Don't use the car to get anywhere in that circle."

**Naismith's Rule:** 1 mile in 20 minutes (add 30 min per 1,000 ft of elevation gain; rarely relevant in cities). Gmap Pedometer (gmap-pedometer.com) for planning.

"Devote a Saturday or Sunday to a cross-town perambulation" changes city perception permanently. Authors walked 26 miles across LA and report it reset their sense of walkable distance.

### Cycling basics (Ch. 7)

Walking/biking/mass-transit triangle — same three-mode framework the URP already uses.

**Cargo bikes:** Authors prefer the **Xtracycle** (a longtail bike extension) over trailers for maneuverability. For very cheap: **DIY bamboo bike trailer** from two used bike wheels — full instructions at carryfreedom.com/bamboo.html.

**Locking:** Two locks — U-lock on rear wheel + frame to a secure object; cable on front wheel + frame. Two locks mean a thief needs two different tools. Secure the wheels specifically or thieves take the rims.

**Folding bikes** for bus/subway compatibility where bikes aren't allowed on board: Bike Friday, Brompton, Dahon (in rough price order). **Beater bike for all-day lockup** is a valid strategy.

### Mass transit siting note (Ch. 7, "Mass Transit")

"When you site your urban homestead, one of the first things you should consider is its proximity to mass transit. The more transit options the better, and the less likely it is that you'll have to rely on a car. Get out the local transit map, and check times and frequency before signing that lease or deed." **This is the URP's "most sustainable transportation decision is a short trip or no trip at all" principle, phrased as a lease-decision criterion.**

### DIY bike light (Ch. 7, "Make Your Own Bike Light")

Malibu halogen floodlight (~$11) + Panasonic sealed lead acid battery (~$20) + inline switch + scavenged copper wire + battery charger + saddlebag. Total ~$35 for a light that matches $100–400 commercial bike lights in performance (runs ~2 hours per charge). *Archived as a potential 03-engineering-projects.md addition if bike commuting becomes central.*

## Extensions, confirmations, and contradictions

**Confirmations** (validate existing URP content at higher resolution):
- "City in a forest" foraging value — confirmed, made concrete
- Energy 40–50% HVAC claim — confirmed with the 49/49 heating/cooling breakdown
- Rain barrel / downspout setup — confirmed; book adds multi-downspout, strainer baskets, metal roof upgrade
- Worm bin using stackable totes — confirmed; book adds the clever migration-harvest mechanism
- Sheet mulching — confirmed and made specific (5"+ organic matter, wet cardboard/newspaper, cut-X-to-plant)
- Aesthetic-first / direct-action principle — independently reached same conclusion
- Perpetual cultures ethic — confirmed and extended
- Three-mode transport triangle — independently aligned

**Contradictions / tensions:**
- **Blueberries and greywater:** The URP currently recommends greywater + blueberries in close Zone 2 adjacency. *The Urban Homestead* explicitly rules out blueberries as greywater incompatible (acid lover in alkaline water). **This is a direct contradiction worth flagging.** Resolution: use rainwater specifically for blueberries; keep greywater zones away from them.
- **Borax in homemade laundry detergent:** The URP's `04-inventories.md` currently lists "grated bar soap + washing soda + borax" as a laundry recipe. The book explicitly lists borax as greywater-incompatible (salt buildup kills plants). Resolution: if using laundry-to-landscape greywater, borax is out; if not, borax is fine. The URP should note this conditional.
- **Kids + buckets:** The book includes a specific warning — "Around 25 kids drown in buckets every year in the US." The URP's manual greywater, shower-bucket, and under-sink-bucket strategies all need this safety note given the project's baby-safe constraint.
