# The Urban Homestead — Highest-Value Contributions to the URP

## The four most valuable things

In order of project impact:

**1. The DIY self-watering container from two 5-gallon buckets.** The single biggest gap in the URP's outdoor-growing infrastructure. SWCs solve both dominant container-gardening failure modes — drought stress in summer heat and root rot from overcompensation — in one design. They cost $0–15 to build from scavenged restaurant buckets. They're fully portable on move-out. Atlanta's humid summers and sudden-drying balcony microclimates make them dramatically higher-yielding than plain pots. This item alone would justify reading the book.

**2. The three-tier mulch framework (regular / sheet / lasagna) with the no-dig principle.** The URP currently gestures at sheet-mulching; *The Urban Homestead* specifies exactly how much material (5+ inches on top of the barrier), in what ratio, layered how, to kill a lawn with zero digging. This is the strongest landlord-negotiation framing the URP has: *"we're adding a foot of organic matter to your yard, which permanently improves drainage and soil health."* Lasagna mulching also provides a method for remediating bad ground with a one-year rest cycle.

**3. The foraging depth.** Acorn processing turns Atlanta's oak canopy into a free staple-calorie source each fall. Kudzu is recast as a Southern food rather than a scourge. The berry hook, the Six Things To Know, the parkway / median / alley guerrilla practice, and the *California v. Greenwood* legal grounding all give the URP practical methodology where it currently only gestures at Concrete Jungle. Atlanta's massive tree canopy and climate make it one of the most forage-rich American cities; the URP is now equipped to actually do the work.

**4. The aesthetic-first landlord approach.** "First Steps Toward An Edible Landscape" (Ch. 1) is the URP's missing tactical doctrine for Zone 2. Not "ask permission to convert the yard" — instead, tuck herbs and radishes around existing spindly shrubs, let the flowers and the beauty do the political work, let the neighbors *see* what's happening. Coyne & Knutzen report zero landlord or municipal friction over two years of their own guerrilla parkway gardening. This reframes the entire landlord conversation from a permission request to a *fait accompli* that improves everything it touches.

## Secondary valuable contributions

- **Bean teepee** as a Zone 1/2 structure that is simultaneously bean-producing, kid-scale play fort, and fully removable
- **Water-use-by-category breakdown** (~59% landscaping, ~11% toilet, ~9% laundry) giving a prioritization frame for household water interventions
- **Six rainwater-harvesting techniques** where the URP currently has one (rain barrels) — depaving, smart gutters, earthworks, mulch basins, terracing, plus IBC tanks for space-efficient storage
- **Atlanta-specific roof yield math:** 26,034 gallons/year from a 992 sq ft asphalt roof at 48.6" annual rainfall
- **Tater-tire stack** for potatoes (20 lbs per stack possible; Atlanta planting window Feb–April)
- **Fertilizer tea** with the "rabbit poop is the only poop safe fresh" rule
- **Soil lead guidance** with the 1% vinegar produce wash and the insight that heavy organic matter *reduces* plant lead uptake
- **Compost-from-a-garbage-can** as a same-day lower-bound option for anyone not ready for worms yet
- **Fil / piima / viila** Scandinavian mesophilic dairy cultures — perpetual cultures that don't need heating, extending the URP's perpetual-culture loop beyond sourdough and kombucha
- **Appalachian Solar Food Dryer** as the cooking-side companion to the haybox
- **The "Nuclear Option"** disinfection (H₂O₂ + vinegar applied separately) for when a household genuinely needs to kill pathogens without chemical warfare

## What the book *doesn't* do

It **confirms** the URP's central posture (act without permission, start with containers, keep notes, build soil, close loops) at higher resolution but doesn't challenge it.

It **pre-dates** the AI-as-sovereignty-tool framing entirely, so `08-knowledge-sharing.md` gets nothing from this book.

It **directly contradicts** the URP in three specific places that need resolution:

1. **Blueberries are not greywater-compatible.** Blueberries require acidic soil (pH 4.5–5.2); greywater is alkaline from soap residues. The URP should route blueberries to rainwater only and send greywater to alkaline-tolerant plants (citrus, figs, blackberries, elderberries, most fruit and nut trees).
2. **Borax in DIY laundry detergent conflicts with laundry-to-landscape greywater.** If routing wash water to plants, borax is out; otherwise it's fine. The URP inventory row needs this conditional.
3. **Bucket safety for young children.** Coyne & Knutzen note ~25 US child bucket-drowning deaths per year. Every bucket-based strategy in the URP (shower warm-up water, under-sink greywater catch, fermentation crocks) needs a child-safety note given the project's baby-safe constraint.
